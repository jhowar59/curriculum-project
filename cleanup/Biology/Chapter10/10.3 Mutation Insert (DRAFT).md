### Insert Slide A — Where does heritable variation come from?

**KEY CONCEPT**
Natural selection acts on variation that already exists — that variation comes from two sources.

- Recall (Ch 6): heritable traits are passed through gametes; only changes in egg or sperm cells reach offspring.
- Heritable variation in a population has **two sources**:
  - **Mutation** — creates brand-new alleles. This is the *original* source of all new variation.
  - **Sexual reproduction** — meiosis (crossing over + independent assortment) and fertilization **shuffle existing alleles** into new combinations. *(This is the Ch 6 material — recombination, not new genes.)*
- Without these, every individual would be identical and natural selection would have nothing to act on.

### Insert Slide B — Mutations create new alleles

- **Definition:** A mutation is a change in the DNA sequence of an organism. *(Recall Ch 8: DNA is the instructions — change the sequence, change the instruction.)*
- Mutations are the **ultimate source of new alleles** — the raw material evolution depends on.
- **Causes of mutations:**
  - random **errors during DNA replication** *(recall Ch 8.3)*
  - **environmental factors** — radiation (UV, X-rays), chemicals (mutagens), some viruses
- Most mutations are **neutral or harmful**, but a few are **beneficial** in a given environment — and those can be favored by natural selection.
- Only mutations in **gametes (germ-line)** are heritable; a mutation in a skin or liver cell is not passed on.

*(Transition into existing slide 9:)* So the "existing variation" natural selection acts on is built by **mutation** (new alleles) and **sexual reproduction** (new combinations). Natural selection then acts on that variation.
