Chapter 11 is the population-genetics half of evolution and the **standards capstone of the whole evolution unit**: genetic variation within populations (11.1), natural selection on trait distributions (11.2), other mechanisms — gene flow, genetic drift, sexual selection (11.3), Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (11.4), speciation (11.5), and patterns in evolution (11.6). It carries the two HS-LS4 PEs Chapter 10 left open: **HS-LS4-3** (use mathematical models to show advantageous traits increase in proportion — the Hardy-Weinberg framework) and **HS-LS4-5** (changes in conditions → speciation and extinction). It also **resolves the mutation question** flagged earlier — see the note below.

> **Correction — mutation IS taught (here, in 11.1).** Earlier notes (from auditing Ch8/Ch10) flagged the mutation source of heritable variation as missing. **That was premature.** `11.1 Notes.pptx` slide 4 teaches it directly: *"Genetic variations come from two main sources: **Mutation** — a random change in DNA, results in a new allele … **Recombination** — new combinations of alleles formed during meiosis."* So **HS-LS4-2**'s mutation factor and **HS-LS3-2**'s mutation prong are covered — in Chapter 11, after natural selection is introduced in Ch10. The committed `Chapter10/10.3 Mutation Insert` is therefore **an enhancement, not a gap-filler**: it pulls the source-of-variation idea forward to the point where 10.3 first says "selection acts on existing variation," instead of waiting for 11.1. Worth keeping for sequencing, but the standard is not unmet.

**Chapter verdict:** This chapter closes out the evolution standards cleanly and is the strongest in the unit for *reasoning*: the 11.5 speciation writing assignment and its companion lesson plan are genuine research-and-argument tasks, not recall. It confirms that **HS-LS4-3 and HS-LS4-5 do land here** (the Ch10 handoff is honored), and it resolves the mutation question (11.1). The one real weakness is narrow and fixable: the unit's *mathematical* standard, HS-LS4-3, is introduced but never practiced.

**Findings worth acting on:**

1. **Hardy-Weinberg is shown but never used — the one genuine HS-LS4-3 gap.** Slide 11.4-5 puts up `p² + 2pq + q² = 1`, but no slide, CFU, or reading ever has students *calculate* with it, and there's no IWS 11.4 at all. HS-LS4-3 is specifically "**use** mathematical models." The fix is a short **HW practice problem**: give a recessive-phenotype frequency (q²), have students find q, p, and the genotype frequencies, then compare predicted vs. observed to judge whether a population is evolving. That single worksheet turns a named equation into the applied math the standard requires. (A peppered-moth / allele-frequency dataset would do the same for 11.2.)

2. **The 11.5 speciation writing assignment is the model the other CFUs should aspire to — leave it alone, but reuse the pattern.** "Define each cause of reproductive isolation, then find a real species that formed that way" is already a claim-evidence task with a research step. It's the best reasoning artifact in the whole audit. No change needed; rather, it's the template for the `[short-answer]` items recommended back in the recall-heavy chapters.

3. **HS-LS4-5 is richly covered** across 11.3 (isolation mechanisms), 11.5 (speciation), and 11.6 (extinction, mass extinction, adaptive radiation), plus the standalone speciation lesson. No gap.

4. **HS-LS4-6 (design a solution to mitigate human impact on biodiversity) is not in the evolution unit** — as expected, it belongs to ecology, which is outside the Ch1–11 set audited here. Noted so the unit isn't faulted for it; verify it lands in the ecology chapters whenever those are reviewed.
