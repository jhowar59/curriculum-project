# Ecology Capstone (DRAFT) — the remaining LS2 standards + LS4-6

**Purpose:** the course stops near Ch11–12 of 23, so the textbook's ecology unit is never reached — but these are **Biology-badged** standards (see `nd-standards-course-badges`). This short capstone shoehorns the LS2 standards that *didn't* fit into Chapter 4 (the carbon-cycle/energy insert handles LS2-5, LS2-4; LS2-3 was already in Ch4). It runs as a brief mini-unit **after evolution (Ch10–11)** — a natural throughline, since evolution already established the *population* as the unit of change and *adaptation* as its product. Ecology just asks: how do those populations behave in ecosystems, and what happens when humans disrupt them?

**Standards covered (all Biology-badged; the first three shared with Env Sci, but Env Sci is not required so Biology still owes them):**
- **HS-LS2-1** — mathematical/computational models for factors affecting **carrying capacity**.
- **HS-LS2-2** — mathematical representations for **population dynamics and biodiversity** (assessment limited to provided data).
- **HS-LS2-6** — evaluate claims/evidence that **interactions keep ecosystems stable, but change can flip them to a new ecosystem**.
- **HS-LS2-7** & **HS-LS4-6** — **design, evaluate, and refine a solution** to reduce human impacts on the environment and biodiversity.
- **HS-LS2-8** — evaluate evidence for the role of **group behavior** in survival and reproduction.

**Footprint:** ~1–1.5 weeks. No equipment (modeling, graph interpretation, and an engineering-design project). Uses the **ND examples the standards themselves cite** — zebra mussels, leafy spurge, CRP grassland, Devils Lake — so it satisfies the "North Dakota Connection" fields too.

## Lesson 1 — Group behavior is an adaptation (HS-LS2-8) *(bridge from evolution)*

### Slide 1
**KEY CONCEPT** Some behaviors evolved because living in a group improves survival and reproduction.
- **Review (Ch10):** natural selection favors heritable traits that raise survival and reproduction — **behavior is a trait too.**
- **Definition:** group behavior is coordinated action by many individuals — flocking, schooling, herding, cooperative hunting, migrating, swarming.

### Slide 2
- Why group behavior is selected for:
  - **Protection** — a school of fish or herd confuses predators; more eyes watching.
  - **Foraging/hunting** — wolves take larger prey as a pack than alone.
  - **Helping relatives** — aiding kin passes on shared genes (recall Ch6 inheritance).
- **Cause & Effect:** the behavior persists because grouped individuals leave **more offspring** than loners.

**CFU 1 task (LS2-8 SEP — evaluate evidence):** Given two examples (e.g. musk ox forming a defensive ring; geese flying in a V), **argue from evidence** whether the group behavior increases individual or species survival, and how. *(short-answer)*

## Lesson 2 — Populations & carrying capacity (HS-LS2-1, HS-LS2-2) *(the math)*

### Slide 3
**KEY CONCEPT** A population grows until its environment limits it.
- **Definition:** a **population** is all the individuals of one species in an area (recall Ch10–11).
- **Definition:** **limiting factors** cap growth — food, water, space, predation, competition, disease.
- **Definition:** **carrying capacity (K)** is the largest population an environment can support over time.

### Slide 4
- **Exponential growth (J-curve):** unlimited resources → population explodes. *(Example: zebra mussels invading a new lake.)*
- **Logistic growth (S-curve):** growth slows and levels off at K as limiting factors kick in.
- **Density-dependent** factors (disease, competition) intensify as a population gets crowded; **density-independent** factors (drought, fire, flood) hit regardless of size.

### Slide 5 *(LS2-1 / LS2-2 SEP — math)*
- Read **K** off a logistic-growth graph (where the curve flattens).
- Interpret population data: find the **trend** and **average**, identify what changed when growth slowed.

**CFU 2 task (math, provided data):** Given a graph of a deer (or zebra-mussel) population over 20 years: (a) estimate the carrying capacity; (b) describe the trend in words; (c) name one likely limiting factor and say whether it's density-dependent or -independent. *(short-answer / calculation; assessment stays within the provided data per the standard)*

## Lesson 3 — Ecosystem stability & change (HS-LS2-6)

### Slide 6
**KEY CONCEPT** Complex interactions keep an ecosystem's numbers fairly steady — until a big enough change resets it.
- **Definition:** **resilience** is an ecosystem's ability to return to normal after a disturbance.
- Stable conditions → relatively constant numbers and types of organisms.
- **Modest disturbance** (seasonal flood, moderate hunting) → ecosystem usually recovers.
- **Extreme disturbance** (volcanic eruption, sea-level rise, an aggressive invasive) → may flip to a **new ecosystem**.

### Slide 7
- **Example (ND):** leafy spurge or zebra mussels outcompete natives and shift the whole community to a new stable state that doesn't bounce back on its own.

**CFU 3 task (LS2-6 SEP — evaluate claims/evidence):** Given a disturbance scenario with data, **evaluate the claim** "this ecosystem will return to its original state." Decide whether the evidence supports recovery or a new ecosystem, and justify. *(short-answer)*

## Lesson 4 / CAPSTONE PROJECT — Design a biodiversity solution (HS-LS2-7 + HS-LS4-6)

### Slide 8
**KEY CONCEPT** Human activity disrupts ecosystems and biodiversity — and engineering design can reduce the harm.
- **Human impacts:** habitat destruction, invasive species, overexploitation, pollution, climate change.
- **Review (Ch11):** biodiversity goes **up** with speciation and **down** with extinction; humans speed extinction.
- **Definition:** biodiversity is the variety of species in an ecosystem; more diversity → more resilience (Lesson 3).

### The project *(the LS2-7 / LS4-6 performance task — the unit's centerpiece)*
Students design, evaluate, and refine a solution to a **real ND biodiversity/human-impact problem**. Suggested problems (all from the standards' ND Connections):
- **Zebra mussels** in ND waters (invasive species control)
- **Leafy spurge** on rangeland (invasive plant)
- **CRP grassland loss** and declining grassland-bird/pollinator diversity
- **Devils Lake** flooding and habitat change

**Engineering-design steps the project must show:**
1. **Define** the problem, with measurable **criteria** (what success looks like) and **constraints** (cost, land, regulations, unintended harm).
2. **Design** a solution grounded in the ecology from Lessons 1–3.
3. **Evaluate tradeoffs** — economic, social, environmental (a built-in part of both standards).
4. **Refine** the design based on a peer/teacher critique.
5. **Present** to the class.

This single project satisfies **both** HS-LS2-7 and HS-LS4-6 (they're near-identical "design a solution for biodiversity" PEs) and pulls the whole capstone together.
