## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§7.3 introduces the classification of chemical reactions into recurring types — the pattern-recognition toolkit that lets students predict products from reactants. Two concept groups:

1. **Classifying Reactions** — Five reaction types defined with general forms and examples:
   - *Synthesis* (A + B → AB): two or more substances combine to form a new compound. General pattern: multiple reactants → single product. Example: 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl. Polymerization is a synthesis variant (many small units join into a polymer chain — polyethene).
   - *Decomposition* (AB → A + B): a compound breaks apart into simpler substances. Example: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (electrolysis). Cracking crude oil is a real-world decomposition example.
   - *Single-displacement* (AX + B → BX + A): one element replaces another in a compound. Example: 3CuCl₂ + 2Al → 2AlCl₃ + 3Cu. A more reactive element displaces a less reactive one.
   - *Double-displacement* (AX + BY → AY + BX): two compounds exchange partners; common results include precipitate formation, gas release, or water formation.
   - *Combustion*: organic compound + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (complete combustion). Incomplete combustion (limited O₂) → CO + soot. Carbon monoxide is colorless, odorless, and toxic.

2. **Electrons and Chemical Reactions** — *Free radicals*: atoms or groups with one unpaired electron, formed when covalent bonds break unevenly (homolytic cleavage). Highly reactive. Ozone in the upper atmosphere is formed and destroyed by radical reactions. *Oxidation-reduction (redox) reactions*: one substance loses electrons (oxidized) and another gains electrons (reduced). Memory device: "OIL RIG" (Oxidation Is Loss; Reduction Is Gain). Combustion and respiration are redox reactions. Rusting (Fe → Fe³⁺ + e⁻) is the textbook's main redox example: iron is oxidized, oxygen is reduced.

## 2 — Cue-column questions (Cornell left column)

### Chunk A — Classifying Reactions
1. What is a synthesis reaction? Write the general form. Give one real example.
2. What is a decomposition reaction? Write the general form. How does it compare to a synthesis reaction?
3. What is a single-displacement reaction? Write the general form. Which element ends up displacing which?
4. What is a double-displacement reaction? Name two possible results of this type of reaction.

### Chunk B — Combustion Reactions
5. What is a combustion reaction? What must always be one of the reactants?
6. Write the general products of a complete combustion reaction for an organic compound.
7. What forms in an *incomplete* combustion reaction? Why is carbon monoxide (CO) dangerous?

### Chunk C — Free Radicals and Redox
8. What is a free radical? How do free radicals form?
9. Define *oxidation* and *reduction* in terms of electrons. (Which gains? Which loses?)
10. What does "redox" stand for? Give one example of a redox reaction from everyday life.
11. In the rusting reaction (Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃), is iron oxidized or reduced? Explain.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Zinc metal (Zn) is added to a solution of copper sulfate (CuSO₄). The following reaction occurs:
>
> Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu
>
> **Claim:** Identify the type of reaction this is. Then predict: if you reversed the experiment and added copper metal (Cu) to zinc sulfate (ZnSO₄), would a reaction occur? **Explain** your prediction using what you know about this reaction type and about which element displaced which. Use the terms *single-displacement*, *reactants*, *products*, and *more reactive* in your answer.
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: reaction type correctly identified, prediction stated, reasoning from single-displacement pattern explained)*

**Scoring note:** Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu is a single-displacement reaction — zinc displaces copper. Prediction: Cu + ZnSO₄ would NOT react (or would react very slowly/not at all) because zinc is more reactive than copper; a less reactive element cannot displace a more reactive one. Students who cannot locate the reactivity reasoning in their notes will make an unsupported guess. In Forms [short-answer] mode, Brisk grades on the 3-part rubric.

**Why this prompt works:** It applies HS-PS1-2 classification to a real reaction not worked in class, then extends to a reactivity prediction — the exact reasoning the HS-PS1-2 clarification statement targets ("predict products of simple reactions"). The reversal question is the hardest part and distinguishes students who understand the pattern from those who memorized the term.
