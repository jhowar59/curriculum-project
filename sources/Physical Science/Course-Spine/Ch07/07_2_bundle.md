## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§7.2 is the chapter's mathematical core and the primary delivery point for HS-PS1-7. Three concept groups:

1. **Describing Reactions — Chemical Equation Notation** — A chemical equation uses symbols to represent a chemical reaction and shows the relationship between reactants (left of arrow) and products (right of arrow). Arrow means "yield." Word equations and molecular models are alternatives, but chemical equations are the universal, precise form.
2. **Balancing Chemical Equations** — A balanced equation has equal atom counts of each element on both sides, satisfying the Law of Conservation of Mass. Coefficients (numbers placed in front of formulas) balance the equation; subscripts within formulas cannot be changed. The step-by-step balancing procedure is the primary procedural skill: (a) write the unbalanced equation; (b) count atoms per element on each side; (c) add/adjust coefficients to equalize; (d) verify. The Mg + O₂ → MgO worked example runs through all steps to reach 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO.
3. **Balanced Equations and Mole Ratios** — A balanced equation encodes the mole ratio (proportion of reactants and products). The 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO equation means 2 mol Mg reacts with 1 mol O₂ (ratio 2:1). Mole ratios can be converted to masses using molar mass (g/mol), following the Law of Definite Proportions. The quantitative worked example (Figure 4 table: 48.6 g Mg + 32.0 g O₂ → 80.6 g MgO) ties the math to conservation of mass numerically.

**Chunk breakdown (3 reading chunks, ~6–8 min each):**

- **Chunk B:** "Balanced Chemical Equations" → conservation of mass, why equations must be balanced, the step-by-step procedure, the Mg/MgO worked example, coefficients vs. subscripts. Stop point: after the Math Skills practice problems. Cue: *What is a coefficient? Why can you change coefficients but not subscripts?*

## 3 — In-class lab block (§7.2 day, ~15 min)

**QuickLab — Balancing Chemical Equations (from textbook, p. 226)**

*Materials:* Toothpicks and gumdrops of at least 3 colors (one color per element type).

*Procedure:*
1. Build gumdrop-and-toothpick models of the reactants in each equation below. Use the same color for the same element throughout.
   - a. H₂ + Cl₂ → HCl
   - b. Ca + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂
   - c. C₂H₆ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
2. Rearrange the atoms of the reactants to form the products. Determine how many of each reactant molecule you need so all atoms can be placed into product molecules — no leftovers, no shortage.

*Analysis:*
1. Write the balanced equation for each reaction based on your gumdrop experiment.
2. Explain: Why can you rearrange atoms but not create or destroy them in this model? Which law does that represent?

*Cornell integration:* Students record the three balanced equations in Chunk B notes and write one sentence connecting the gumdrop model to the Law of Conservation of Mass. This sentence becomes evidence they can use on the CER.

*Equipment:* Toothpicks (standard); gumdrops or modeling clay in 3+ colors. No safety concerns.

## 4 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Iron (Fe) reacts with oxygen gas (O₂) to form iron(III) oxide, also known as rust (Fe₂O₃). The unbalanced equation is:
>
> Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃
>
> **Part A:** Balance this equation. Show your atom counts before and after adding coefficients.
>
> **Part B:** Explain how your balanced equation demonstrates the Law of Conservation of Mass. Use the terms *coefficient*, *reactants*, *products*, and *conservation of mass* in your explanation.
>
> *(3–5 sentences for Part B; open own notes; graded on: correctly balanced equation, atom counts verified, conservation of mass explained conceptually)*

**Scoring note:** Balanced equation: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃. Part A: Fe left = 4, right = 4; O left = 6, right = 6. Part B: students must state that equal atom counts on both sides means no atoms were created or destroyed — the balanced equation is proof that mass is conserved. Students using gumdrop notes or the Mg worked example to scaffold their answer are working exactly as intended.

**Why this prompt works:** It applies the Chunk B balancing procedure to a new, everyday reaction (rust) that was not worked in class, directly targeting HS-PS1-7 ("use mathematical representations to provide evidence that atoms, and therefore mass, are conserved during a chemical reaction"). Part B forces the conceptual link — not just the mechanics.
