## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§7.4 explains why some reactions proceed faster than others and introduces catalysts and equilibrium as tools for controlling reactions. Three concept groups:

1. **Factors Affecting Reaction Rates** — Rate increases when particle contact increases. Five factors:
   - *Temperature*: higher temperature → faster-moving particles → more frequent collisions and more collisions with enough energy (activation energy). Example: cooking speeds up at 200 °C vs. 100 °C.
   - *Concentration*: more particles available → more collisions → faster reaction.
   - *Surface area*: smaller pieces expose more inner surface → more contact. Example: sliced potato cooks faster than whole.
   - *Particle mass/size/shape*: massive molecules move slower (kinetic theory); large bulky molecules must align correctly to react, so they react more slowly.
   - *Pressure* (for gases): higher pressure → particles squeezed into smaller space → closer together → more collisions.

2. **Catalysts** — A catalyst speeds up or slows down a reaction without being consumed or changed significantly. Inhibitors slow reactions. Solid catalysts work by providing a surface where reactants can collect, increasing contact. Catalytic converter (platinum/palladium honeycomb) converts toxic exhaust gases to harmless products. *Enzymes* are biological catalysts: protein (or RNA) molecules. *Substrate* = the specific reactant an enzyme acts on. *Active site* = region on the enzyme that fits only a specific substrate (lock-and-key specificity). *Catalase* = enzyme that breaks down H₂O₂ into H₂O + O₂ (found in liver and potato).

3. **Equilibrium Systems** — Some reactions are reversible: can go in both directions (double arrow). When the forward and reverse rates become equal, the system reaches *chemical equilibrium* — no net change, but both reactions continue. Le Châtelier's principle: if a change is applied to a system at equilibrium, the equilibrium shifts to oppose the change until a new equilibrium is reached. Factors: temperature (favors endothermic/exothermic direction), pressure (favors fewer gas molecules), concentration (favors production of less of the added substance). The Haber process (N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ + energy) is the industrial application example.

## 3 — In-class lab block (§7.4 day, ~30 min)

**Inquiry Lab — What Affects the Rates of Chemical Reactions? (from textbook)**

*Materials:* Paper clip, steel wool, Bunsen burner or hot plate with flame, tongs; vinegar (acetic acid), water, magnesium ribbon; 6 test tubes, labels; small beakers of ice-cold water and hot water.

*Procedure:*
1. Using tongs, hold a paper clip in the hottest part of a burner flame for 30 s; repeat with a ball of steel wool. Record observations. (Tests surface area: paper clip = minimal surface; steel wool = enormous surface.)
2. Label test tubes A, B, C. Put 10 mL vinegar in A; 5 mL vinegar + 5 mL water in B; 2.5 mL vinegar + 7.5 mL water in C. Add a piece of magnesium ribbon to each. Record observations. (Tests concentration: A = highest concentration of acid.)
3. Label test tubes 1, 2, 3. Put 5 mL vinegar + 5 mL water in each. Submerge tube 1 in ice-cold water; leave tube 2 at room temperature; submerge tube 3 in hot water. Add magnesium ribbon to each. Record observations. (Tests temperature.)

*Analysis:*
1. Describe and interpret your results for each step. Which test tube in each group reacted fastest?
2. For each step, identify the factor(s) that influenced the rate of reaction.
3. (Extension) Hypothesis: how would results differ if the experiment were carried out at a different temperature? Design the experiment to test it — list steps, identify variables kept constant.

*Cornell integration:* Students write their factor-specific observations as Cornell note entries (one Q per factor tested: "What happens to the rate when concentration doubles?"). These notes are directly usable on the CER.

*Equipment notes:* Vinegar = standard household white vinegar; magnesium ribbon (check INVENTORY for stock); steel wool (hardware store). Bunsen burner or alcohol lamp; ice available from faculty room or requested in advance; hot water from tap or hot plate.

> **Prompt:**

> A catalytic converter uses platinum metal as a catalyst to convert carbon monoxide (CO) into carbon dioxide (CO₂). The platinum is a solid metal surface and is not consumed in the reaction.
>
> **Explain:** (a) why the platinum speeds up the reaction without being used up, and (b) how this is an example of a solid catalyst providing a surface for reactants to meet. Also identify what type of reaction the conversion of CO to CO₂ represents (from §7.3). Use the terms *catalyst*, *surface area*, *reactants*, and *active site* in your answer.
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: catalyst mechanism explained, surface area connection made, reaction type identified)*

**Scoring note:** (a) Platinum provides a surface where CO and O₂ molecules collect and react; the platinum is not incorporated into the products, so it remains available. (b) The large honeycomb surface maximizes contact between CO and O₂ molecules. Reaction type: combustion (CO + O₂ → CO₂). The term "active site" is in students' Chunk B notes from enzyme content — it earns its place here only if students can transfer the "specific fit" concept; partial credit if they use it in a related but not exact way. In Forms [short-answer] mode, Brisk grades on the 3-part rubric.

**Why this prompt works:** It connects §7.4 catalyst content to a real-world object (the catalytic converter described in the sub-deck), requires transferring the solid-catalyst surface mechanism from the enzyme active-site model, and pulls in §7.3 reaction type classification — a cross-section synthesis that matches the chapter's capstone goal.
