## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§2.3 closes the Ch2 chemistry foundation with two major concept groups and a practical applications layer:

1. **Physical Changes** — defined as changes affecting physical properties without changing substance identity; examples: breaking chalk, dissolving sugar, sanding wood, melting gold; key insight — arrangement of atoms does not change; dissolving is explicitly classified as a physical change (sugar molecules spread out but do not become new molecules).
2. **Chemical Changes** — defined as one or more substances changed into entirely new substances with different properties; signs of chemical change (the clip names: color change, gas/bubbles production, energy release/absorption); everyday examples: battery dying, cellular respiration, fruit ripening, digestion, leaf color change, Statue of Liberty patina; foreshadows energy strand — energy is absorbed or released.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Your friend watches you add baking soda and vinegar together. She says, "That's just mixing — you're making a mixture." You say it's a chemical change. Using your notes, **support your claim** that this is a chemical change and not simply a mixture being made. In your answer: (1) state your claim clearly; (2) identify at least two signs of a chemical change that you could observe in this reaction; (3) explain what those signs tell you about what happened to the substances — are the products the same as the starting materials?
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: claim stated, two signs identified with reasoning, conclusion about product identity)*

**Scoring note (paper + Forms/Brisk mode):** Signs to credit: bubbling/gas production (CO₂ released), temperature change (endothermic — solution feels cold), possible color change. Products differ from reactants — this is the definition of a chemical change. The report card identifies this exact prompt as the chapter's priority bolt-on CER. Brisk can grade the 3-part rubric. Paper: check for claim + two signs + product identity conclusion.

**Why this prompt works:** It is the HS-PS1-3 evidence task — using observable indicators to argue that a substance identity change occurred. It directly previews the endo/exo lab (baking soda + vinegar is Experiment 1 in the 2026 lab). Students who took good notes during Chunk B can answer all three parts; students who only remember "chemical change = something happened" will miss the product identity explanation.
