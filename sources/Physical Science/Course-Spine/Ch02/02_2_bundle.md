## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A crime scene investigator finds an unknown white powder. She measures its mass (18.9 g) and volume (10.0 cm³), calculates its density, and compares it to a reference table. She also notes that the powder does NOT react with vinegar. Using your notes, **explain how she would use physical and chemical properties together to identify the substance.** In your answer: (1) calculate the density of the powder and show your work; (2) explain whether density is a physical or chemical property and why it helps identify substances; (3) explain what the "no reaction with vinegar" observation tells her, and whether that is a physical or chemical property.
>
> *(3–5 sentences plus the calculation; open own notes; graded on: correct density calculated, property types correctly identified with reasoning, claim about identification stated)*

**Scoring note (paper + Forms/Brisk mode):** D = 18.9 g ÷ 10.0 cm³ = 1.89 g/cm³. Density is a physical property — observable/measurable without changing the substance's identity. "No reaction with vinegar" is a chemical property — it describes behavior only visible when a change in identity could occur (nonflammability/reactivity category). Brisk can grade the 3-part rubric. Paper: check calculation, two property types, identification claim.

**Why this prompt works:** It is a direct HS-PS1-3 task — using characteristic properties (physical + chemical together) to identify an unknown substance. The calculation is §2.2's quantitative anchor; the "no reaction" observation mirrors the Reactivity QuickLab. Students with good notes can reconstruct both answers; guessers cannot calculate the density correctly without the equation.

## 4 — In-class lab block (QuickLabs from §2.2)

**Lab A title:** Density of Water (embedded QuickLab, §2.2)

**Materials:**
- 100 mL graduated cylinder
- 250 mL beaker with water
- Balance
- Graph paper or graphing calculator

**Procedure:**
- Find the mass of the empty 100 mL graduated cylinder.
- Pour 10 mL of water from the beaker into the graduated cylinder. Find the mass of cylinder + water.
- Repeat for several different volumes (20 mL, 30 mL, 40 mL, etc.).
- Plot volume (x-axis) vs. mass (y-axis) on graph paper.

**Analysis questions:**
1. Estimate the mass of 55 mL and 85 mL of water from your graph.
2. Predict the volume of 25 g and 75 g of water.
3. Use the slope of your graph to determine the density of water.

**Cornell integration:** Students record the data table in their notes and write D = m/V and the calculated density value directly in the Chunk B note space. The graph slope = 1.0 g/cm³ provides a memorable empirical anchor for density of water.

---

**Lab B title:** Reactivity (embedded QuickLab, §2.2)

**Materials:**
- 4 g each of baking soda (compound A) and powdered sugar (compound B)
- Two clear plastic cups
- 5 mL vinegar (per cup)
- Balance

**Procedure:**
- Measure 4 g each of compound A and compound B. Place in separate cups.
- Observe color and texture of each. Record observations.
- Add 5 mL of vinegar to each cup. Record observations.
- Identify which compound is baking soda and which is powdered sugar based on the results.

**Debrief question:** Baking soda reacted with vinegar; powdered sugar did not. Is the ability to react with vinegar a physical property or a chemical property? How do you know?

**Cornell integration:** Students write this in the Chunk C note space: "reactivity = chemical property — observed only when identity might change." This is the direct evidence they will use in the §2.3 CER.
