## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§2.1 builds the classification vocabulary the entire chemistry strand depends on. Three major concept groups:

1. **What Is Matter / Elements** — matter defined (mass + takes up space); element defined (cannot be broken down by chemical means); atom as smallest unit keeping element's properties; element symbols (one or two letters, worldwide convention); the periodic table as the master reference.
2. **Compounds and Chemical Formulas** — compound as two or more elements chemically bonded; fixed composition (water always H₂O); chemical formula notation (subscripts for atom counts, coefficients for molecule counts); compound properties differ from component elements (nylon example; water vs. H₂ and O₂).
3. **Pure Substances and Mixtures** — pure substance defined (fixed composition, definite properties); mixture defined (not chemically combined, physically separable); homogeneous vs. heterogeneous mixtures; miscible vs. immiscible liquids; gas-liquid mixtures (carbonated drinks, foams).

**QuickLab embedded — "Mystery Mixture" (chromatography):** The section contains a paper chromatography lab (coffee filter strip + black marker + water). See lab block (§4 below).

**Chunk breakdown (3 reading chunks, ~6–8 min each):**
- **Chunk A:** "What Is Matter?" through "Elements" (including element symbols and the human body figure). Stop: after the paragraph on atoms joining to make molecules. Cue: *What is the difference between an atom and an element? Can an element be broken down further by chemical means?*

- **Chunk C:** "Pure Substances and Mixtures" through the end (homo/heterogeneous, miscible/immiscible, gas-liquid foams). Stop: end of section. Cue: *What is the difference between a homogeneous mixture and a heterogeneous mixture? Give one example of each from the text.*

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A student claims that table salt (NaCl) and pure sodium (Na) are "basically the same substance because they both contain sodium." Using your notes, **argue for or against this claim.** In your answer: (1) state whether NaCl is an element, a compound, or a mixture and explain how you know; (2) use the concept of chemical bonding and fixed composition to explain why NaCl's properties differ from pure sodium; (3) classify your answer using at least two vocabulary terms from today's Key Terms.
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: correct classification stated, reasoning using fixed composition or chemical bonding, at least two terms used correctly)*

**Scoring note (paper + Forms/Brisk mode):** Correct answer — NaCl is a compound; properties differ because atoms are chemically bonded in fixed proportions (2:1 or in this case 1:1 Na:Cl), not just mixed. Students who confuse mixture with compound or element with atom will miss this. Brisk can assess the 3-part rubric. Paper: scan for "compound," one property difference, two terms.

**Why this prompt works:** It targets HS-PS1-1 (elements and compounds via the periodic table) and the HS-PS1-3 foundation (classification as the precondition for using characteristic properties). The Na vs. NaCl contrast is vivid and memorable — sodium explodes in water; salt dissolves harmlessly. Good notes = can answer all three parts; thin notes = stuck on part 2.

## 4 — In-class lab block (Mystery Mixture — paper chromatography)

**Lab title:** Mystery Mixture (embedded QuickLab, §2.1)

**Materials:**
- Pencil
- Clear plastic cup
- Scissors
- Coffee filter strip (3 cm × 15 cm)
- Clear tape
- Water-soluble black marker
- Water (1 cm depth in cup)

**Procedure:**
- Place pencil across the top of the cup. Cut a coffee filter strip (3 cm × 15 cm) and wrap one end around the pencil; tape in place.
- Remove paper from cup. Use the black marker to make a dot in the center of the strip, 2 cm from the bottom.
- Pour water into the cup to a depth of 1 cm.
- Lower the paper into the cup so the dot is above the waterline. Rest the pencil on the rim.
- Remove the paper when the water front is 1 cm from the top. Record observations.

**Debrief question:** The black ink separated into multiple colors. What does this tell you about whether black ink is an element, a compound, or a mixture? How does the ability to physically separate it (by water movement) connect to today's definition of mixture?

**Cornell integration:** Students draw and label the chromatography strip in their notes (bands of color = components of the mixture) directly after the Chunk C reading on heterogeneous vs. homogeneous mixtures. Label: "physical separation = mixture, not compound."
