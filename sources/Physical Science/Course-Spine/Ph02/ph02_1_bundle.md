## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§ph02.1 is the HS-PS2-1 delivery section — the section where F = ma becomes quantitative. Three concept clusters:

1. **Newton's 1st Law (formalized)** — inertia reviewed; net force = 0 means no acceleration (not no motion); an object in uniform motion stays in uniform motion unless acted on by an unbalanced force; inertia is a property of mass, not force.
2. **Newton's 2nd Law (F = ma)** — net force = mass × acceleration; greater force = greater acceleration (if mass constant); greater mass = smaller acceleration (if force constant); solving for any variable; units: 1 N = 1 kg·m/s². The section includes multiple worked examples and the "two sled" diagram (equal force, different masses → different accelerations).
3. **Applications** — calculating acceleration from force and mass; calculating force needed to achieve a given acceleration; the air-bag design connection (HS-PS2-3 preview).

## 2 — Cue-column questions (Cornell left column)

### Chunk A — Newton's 1st Law (Inertia)
1. State Newton's 1st Law in your own words.
2. What is inertia? Does every object have it? How is it related to mass?
3. A bowling ball rolls down a frictionless lane forever. Which law explains this? What net force is acting on it?
4. Why do passengers slide forward when a car stops suddenly? Use the terms "net force" and "inertia."

### Chunk B — Newton's 2nd Law (F = ma)
5. Write Newton's 2nd Law as a formula. Define each variable and its unit.
6. If you push two sleds with the same force but one sled has more mass, which accelerates faster? Why?
7. A net force of 30 N acts on a 6 kg cart. Calculate the acceleration.
8. A 4 kg ball accelerates at 5 m/s². What net force is acting on it?

### Chunk C — Applying F = ma and Air Bags
9. Rearrange F = ma to solve for mass. A net force of 50 N causes an acceleration of 2.5 m/s². What is the mass?
10. Why does an air bag save lives in a collision? How does increasing the stopping distance affect the force?
11. (Challenge) A 1,200 kg car needs to accelerate at 3 m/s². What net force is required?

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Two students test Newton's 2nd Law. They push a 3 kg cart on a frictionless surface with different forces and record the acceleration each time:
>
> | Trial | Net Force (N) | Acceleration (m/s²) |
> |-------|---------------|----------------------|
> | 1     | 6             | 2.0                  |
> | 2     | 12            | 4.0                  |
> | 3     | 18            | 6.0                  |
>
> **Claim:** State the mathematical relationship between net force and acceleration for a constant mass (write it as a proportionality and as the F = ma equation). **Evidence:** Use the data table above to support your claim — show that the relationship holds for all three trials. **Reasoning:** Predict what acceleration the cart would have if the net force were 24 N. Show the calculation using F = ma.
>
> *(Open own notes; graded on: claim states F ∝ a at constant mass, all three F = ma checks shown, prediction calculation correct)*

**Scoring note:** Each trial: a = F/m → 6/3 = 2.0, 12/3 = 4.0, 18/3 = 6.0 ✓. Prediction: 24/3 = 8.0 m/s². Students who write "force doubled and acceleration doubled" without calculation get partial credit — require the equation. This prompt mirrors the HS-PS2-1 data-analysis task exactly.

**Why this prompt works:** It mirrors the HS-PS2-1 performance expectation (analyze data to support the claim that F = ma). The data table format trains the skill students will use on any assessment that tests this standard. Same-day open notes means they can reference their F = ma work from Chunk B.
