## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§ph02.3 is the HS-PS2-2 and HS-PS2-3 primary delivery. Two major concept clusters:

1. **Newton's 3rd Law — force pairs** — every force has an equal and opposite reaction force; action and reaction forces are always equal in size and opposite in direction; the forces act on *different* objects (so they don't cancel each other); examples: foot on ball/ball on foot, swimmer on water/water on swimmer, Earth pulling ball/ball pulling Earth; rockets (hot gases pushed backward → rocket pushed forward).
2. **Momentum** — momentum p = mv (vector: direction = direction of velocity); SI unit = kg·m/s; heavier or faster objects have more momentum; conservation of momentum: total momentum of a system stays constant when no net external force acts (collisions, explosions); the car-vs-truck braking example.

2. Action and reaction forces are equal and opposite — so why don't they cancel each other?

7. A 200 kg motorcycle moves at 15 m/s. Calculate its momentum. How does this compare to the car? Why?

### Chunk C — Conservation of Momentum
9. State the law of conservation of momentum. Under what condition does it apply?

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Car manufacturers design crumple zones in the front of cars to help protect passengers during collisions. In a crash, the crumple zone deforms slowly, increasing the time it takes for the car to stop. **Claim:** Using Newton's 3rd Law, explain what forces act between the car and the obstacle (e.g., a wall) during the collision. **Evidence:** Use the impulse-momentum concept (if momentum change is fixed, then longer stopping time → smaller force: F × t = Δp) to explain quantitatively how increasing stopping time reduces the force on the passenger. **Reasoning:** Explain why a crumple zone is a better safety design than a rigid bumper, using force, time, and HS-PS2-3 vocabulary (minimize force on object during collision).
>
> *(Open own notes; 4–5 sentences; graded on: Newton's 3rd Law force pair named, force-time relationship stated, crumple zone advantage vs. rigid bumper explained with physics reasoning)*

**Scoring note:** Newton's 3rd Law: car exerts force on wall → wall exerts equal and opposite force on car (and passengers). Impulse-momentum: F × Δt = Δ(mv); Δ(mv) is fixed by collision speed and mass; increasing Δt (crumple time) decreases F. Crumple zone advantage: spreads the stopping time over more distance/time → reduces peak force on passenger. Rigid bumper = short stopping time = very high peak force. This is the HS-PS2-3 engineering application.

**Why this prompt works:** It directly addresses the HS-PS2-3 performance expectation (apply Newton's 1st and 3rd Laws to design a device that minimizes force during collision). The crumple zone is the real-world application; the impulse connection gives it quantitative grounding that goes slightly beyond what the textbook states, but is accessible with their F = ma and momentum notes.

## 4 — In-class lab block (Action and Reaction Forces — QuickLab)

**Lab title:** Action and Reaction Forces (embedded QuickLab, §ph02.3)

**Materials per pair:**
- 2 spring scales
- 1 × 2 kg mass (or textbook)
- String/hook connections

**Procedure:**
1. Hang the 2 kg mass from one spring scale. Record the reading (this is the weight = action force of Earth pulling the mass down, reaction = mass pulling Earth up — also equal to normal reading).
2. Keep the mass connected to Scale 1. Link Scale 2 to Scale 1 so Scale 1 hangs below Scale 2 (Scale 2 → Scale 1 → mass).
3. Record the reading on both scales.

**Analysis questions:**
1. What are the action and reaction forces in this system?
2. How did the two spring scale readings compare? What does this demonstrate about Newton's 3rd Law?
3. (Challenge) If you added another scale in series (Scale 3 → Scale 2 → Scale 1 → mass), what would each scale read? Why?

**Cornell integration:** Students sketch the two-scale diagram, label action/reaction force arrows, and write one sentence: "Action force = ___ N; Reaction force = ___ N; Newton's 3rd Law confirmed because ___."
