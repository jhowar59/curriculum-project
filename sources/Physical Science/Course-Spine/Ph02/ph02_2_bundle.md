## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§ph02.2 applies Newton's 2nd Law to the gravitational force, extending F = ma to the specific case where the force is gravity. Four concept clusters:

1. **Weight and mass** — weight is the gravitational force on an object (w = mg); mass is the amount of matter (constant anywhere); weight varies by location (moon, Mars); weight is measured in newtons; the astronaut-on-moon example (same mass, less weight).
2. **Law of universal gravitation** — every mass attracts every other mass; gravitational force increases with mass, decreases with the square of distance; G is the universal constant; the Earth-moon-sun system.
3. **Free fall** — when gravity is the only force, all objects fall at the same rate near Earth (g = 9.8 m/s² downward) regardless of mass; the counterintuitive result follows directly from F = ma (heavier object = more gravitational force AND more mass to accelerate — the two effects cancel).
4. **Terminal velocity** — when air resistance balances weight (friction force = gravitational force), acceleration stops; sky diver example; parachute reduces terminal velocity by increasing surface area and air resistance.
5. **Projectile motion** — horizontal and vertical motions are independent; thrown objects follow a curved path because horizontal velocity is constant while vertical free-fall acceleration acts downward.

## 2 — Cue-column questions (Cornell left column)

### Chunk A — Weight, Mass, and Gravity
1. What is the difference between mass and weight? Which is the same everywhere in the universe?
2. Write the formula for weight. What are the units for weight? For mass?
3. A 70 kg person stands on Earth (g = 9.8 m/s²). Calculate their weight in newtons.
4. The same person travels to the moon (g = 1.6 m/s²). What is their weight there? Has their mass changed?
5. Why does weight depend on location but mass does not?

### Chunk B — Universal Gravitation and Free Fall
6. State Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation in words. What two factors affect the size of the gravitational force?
7. All objects near Earth fall at the same rate (g = 9.8 m/s²) when air resistance is ignored. Use F = ma to explain WHY a heavier object doesn't fall faster.
8. Is there gravity in space (far from any planet or star)? Why or why not?

### Chunk C — Terminal Velocity and Projectile Motion
9. What is terminal velocity? What two forces are equal when a falling object reaches it?
10. Why does opening a parachute reduce a sky diver's terminal velocity?
11. In projectile motion, which component of velocity changes (increases) as the object falls? Which stays constant? Why?

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A tennis ball (0.06 kg) and a bowling ball (5 kg) are dropped simultaneously from the same height in a vacuum. **Claim:** State which ball hits the ground first (or if they land at the same time). **Evidence:** Show the calculation of gravitational force on each ball (F = mg, g = 9.8 m/s²). Then use Newton's 2nd Law (a = F/m) to calculate the free-fall acceleration of each ball. **Reasoning:** Use your calculations to explain why the two balls hit at the same time despite the huge difference in their gravitational forces.
>
> *(Open own notes; 2 calculations each + 2 reasoning sentences; graded on: both land simultaneously, both F = mg correct, both a = F/m correct showing a = g for both, reasoning explains the cancellation)*

**Scoring note:** Tennis ball: F = 0.06 × 9.8 = 0.588 N; a = 0.588/0.06 = 9.8 m/s². Bowling ball: F = 5 × 9.8 = 49 N; a = 49/5 = 9.8 m/s². Both land at the same time. Reasoning: larger mass gets more gravitational force, but more mass also requires more force to accelerate — the two effects cancel exactly, leaving the same acceleration for all masses. This is the cleanest HS-PS2-1 application (F = ma applied to gravity).

**Why this prompt works:** The "same fall time" result is counterintuitive, so students with thin understanding will guess wrong. The calculation structure forces them to see WHY the result works through Newton's 2nd Law. The reasoning sentence asks for the mechanism, not just the answer — exactly the evidence+reasoning structure of HS-PS2-1 assessment tasks.
