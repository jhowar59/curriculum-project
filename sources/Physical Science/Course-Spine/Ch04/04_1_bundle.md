## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**

Teachers should introduce the historical development of atomic theory (atomic theory revised by Thomson's and Rutherford's experiments, the nucleus concept) before assigning this reading, or use the Ch4 Part 1 deck's opening slides (4.1 content) to cover that ground first. Students need to know what a nucleus is before this reading will make sense.

Three main concept groups:

1. **What Is in an Atom?** — The three subatomic particles (proton, neutron, electron), their charges, masses, and locations. The nucleus (protons + neutrons at center); electrons orbiting outside. Most atoms are electrically neutral because proton and electron counts balance. The electric force holds the atom together.

2. **Atomic Number and Mass Number** — Atomic number Z = number of protons (defines the element; never changes). Mass number A = protons + neutrons (can vary within an element). Isotopes: same Z, different A, different number of neutrons; same chemical properties but different masses. Three hydrogen isotopes (protium, deuterium, tritium) as the canonical example. Includes a **QuickLab** — Modeling Isotopes with gumdrops (OCR is fragmented; see lab block below).

3. **Atomic Masses, AMU, and the Mole** — Unified atomic mass unit (u): 1/12 the mass of carbon-12. Avogadro's number: 6.022 × 10²³ particles per mole. Molar mass: mass in grams of one mole; equals the average atomic mass in u. Average atomic mass is a weighted average across naturally occurring isotopes (e.g., carbon is 12.01 u, not exactly 12.00 u, because natural carbon includes C-14 etc.). Moles-to-grams conversion.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Carbon has two naturally occurring isotopes: carbon-12 (6 protons, 6 neutrons) and carbon-13 (6 protons, 7 neutrons). The average atomic mass of carbon listed on the periodic table is **12.01 u**, not exactly 12.00 u.
>
> **Claim:** Are carbon-12 and carbon-13 the same element? Explain why or why not.  
> **Evidence and reasoning:** Use your notes to explain (a) what makes them isotopes rather than different elements, and (b) why the average atomic mass is 12.01 u instead of 12.00 u. Use at least two vocabulary terms from today's reading (atomic number, mass number, isotope, molar mass, or unified atomic mass unit).
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: correct claim about element identity, isotope reasoning tied to atomic number, explanation of weighted average mass)*

**Scoring note:** Key elements — (1) same element because same Z (6 protons); (2) different isotopes because different number of neutrons / different A; (3) average atomic mass is a weighted average across isotopes in nature, so it's not a whole number. Students who wrote only definitions will struggle with (3) — this is the payoff for taking good Chunk C notes. Paper: holistic scan for claim + Z reasoning + average mass explanation. Forms [short-answer] mode: Brisk rubric on three-part claim-evidence-reasoning.

**Why this prompt works:** It applies both Chunk B (isotopes, Z vs. A) and Chunk C (average atomic mass as weighted average) in a single scenario. The carbon-12/carbon-13 pair is simpler than hydrogen's three isotopes, so the reasoning task is clean. Good notes → full answer; definition-only notes → incomplete reasoning on part (b).

## 4 — In-class lab block (§4.2 day, QuickLab ~15–20 min)

**QuickLab — Modeling Isotopes** *(p. 122, Holt Science Spectrum; OCR reconstruction below)*

**Materials:** Gumdrops — three colors (one color = protons, one color = neutrons, one color = electrons); toothpicks to connect nuclear particles together. Each group needs ~10 gumdrops + 5–6 toothpicks.

**Procedure (reconstructed from fragmented OCR):**
1. Assign colors: e.g., red = proton, green = neutron, yellow = electron. Record your key.
2. Build the three isotopes of hydrogen:
   - Protium (A = 1): 1 proton, 0 neutrons, 1 electron
   - Deuterium (A = 2): 1 proton, 1 neutron, 1 electron
   - Tritium (A = 3): 1 proton, 2 neutrons, 1 electron
3. Build helium-3 (A = 3) and helium-4 (A = 4) nuclei.
4. Place models side by side for the Analysis questions.

**Analysis questions (from OCR, reconstructed):**
1. How do the three hydrogen isotopes compare to one another? What is the same? What is different?
2. How do the hydrogen isotopes differ from the helium isotopes?
3. Tritium (H-3, A = 3) and helium-3 (He-3, A = 3) have the same mass number. Are they isotopes of the same element? Explain.

**Cornell integration:** After the lab, students add one note to their Chunk B section: "Isotopes = same # of [protons / Z], different # of [neutrons / A]." The lab makes this tactile before the abstract AMU content in Chunk C.

**Lab timing:** This fits between Chunks B and C (~15 min). Students can eat the gumdrops at the end — the edible payoff improves buy-in.

## 5 — Bell-ringer CFU (NEXT DAY, closed notes)

*Use the opening of the §4.3 class day to close the loop on §4.2.*

- A. An atom has 11 protons and 12 neutrons. What is its atomic number? What is its mass number?
- B. Oxygen-16 and oxygen-18 are both oxygen. What is the same about them, and what is different? What are atoms like these called?
- C. (Harder) The average atomic mass of chlorine is 35.45 u, even though individual chlorine atoms are either Cl-35 or Cl-37 (both whole numbers). Explain why the average is not a whole number.

*In Forms mode: A is [auto-grade] if answer-matched (Z = 11, A = 23); B is [short-answer]; C is [short-answer]. On paper: all three hand-graded. Expected answers — A: Z = 11, A = 23; B: same atomic number / number of protons (Z = 8), different mass number / number of neutrons; called isotopes; C: average atomic mass is a weighted average of Cl-35 and Cl-37 in their natural proportions — more Cl-35 than Cl-37 pulls the average below 36.*
