## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**

> **OCR / sequencing note:** §4.1 (Development of Atomic Theory — Democritus, Dalton, Thomson's cathode-ray experiment, Rutherford's nuclear model) is **absent from the OCR**. Teachers should ensure students have covered both the historical atomic models (§4.1 — use the Ch4 Part 1 deck) and the subatomic particle / isotope content (§4.2, covered in the Ch04 `04_1_bundle.md`) before assigning this reading.

Three main concept groups:

1. **Modern Models of the Atom** — The Bohr model (1913): electrons exist only in discrete energy levels (not between them); gain energy to move up, lose energy to move down. The "stairless building" analogy — electrons can be on any floor but not between floors. The modern model refines Bohr: electron location cannot be predicted precisely (electron cloud / probability model).

2. **Electron Energy Levels and Orbitals** — How energy levels are filled in sequence. Each level contains orbitals by type: s (1 orbital = 2 electrons), p (3 orbitals = 6 electrons), d (5 orbitals = 10 electrons). Level 1: 2 electrons; Level 2: 8; Level 3: 18; Level 4: 32 (from the orbital capacity table). Valence electrons: electrons in the outermost filled energy level; these determine chemical properties. A **QuickLab** (Electron Levels — pennies as electrons, table as orbital map) is embedded in the OCR.

3. **Electron Transitions** — Ground state (lowest energy; normal temperature). Excited state (electron gains energy by absorbing a photon; jumps to higher level). Electron falls back, releases a photon. The energy of the emitted photon determines the color of light (fireworks application). Electrons can jump between any two levels, not just adjacent ones.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Fireworks produce different colors of light — red, green, blue — depending on which metal compounds are packed inside the shell. When the firework explodes, the metal atoms absorb energy from the heat.
>
> **Using your notes from today's reading, explain in 3–5 sentences:** What happens to the electrons in a metal atom when the firework explodes, and why do those electrons produce a specific color of light rather than a mix of all colors? Use the terms *energy level*, *photon*, *ground state*, and *excited state* in your answer.
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: electrons described moving from ground → excited state by absorbing energy; photon emitted when electron falls back; specific color tied to specific energy gap between levels)*

**Scoring note:** Key elements — (1) electrons absorb energy and jump from ground state to excited state; (2) electrons fall back to a lower level and emit a photon; (3) the specific color/energy of the photon is determined by the size of the energy gap between the two levels, and that gap is fixed for a given element — so each element emits a characteristic color. Students whose notes include the photon definition and the ground/excited state distinction can answer all three parts. Paper: holistic scan for the three-part chain. Forms [short-answer] mode: Brisk rubric on the three-step electron transition sequence.

**Why this prompt works:** The fireworks connection is explicitly in the textbook's "Why It Matters" intro for §4.3, so students who read the opener are already primed. The CER requires chaining three concepts (ground state → photon absorbed → excited state → photon emitted → specific color) in sequence, which tests whether the student has the causal structure or just isolated vocabulary. Good notes → full answer; vocab-only notes → claim without mechanism.

## 4 — In-class lab block (§4.3 day, QuickLab ~20 min)

**QuickLab — Electron Levels** *(p. 131, Holt Science Spectrum; partially reconstructed from OCR)*

**Materials:** Grid paper or a pre-drawn table (10 columns × 4 rows); pennies (~20 per group) as "electrons"; pencil.

**Setup:**
- Label the first cell "Energy level." Label the first row's remaining cells by orbital type: **s** (1 cell), **p** (3 cells), **d** (5 cells). (The table models orbital slots — one cell = one orbital = two electrons max, but the pennies represent single electrons for simplicity.)
- Label the first column rows 1, 2, 3 (energy levels).
- Cross out cells that correspond to orbital types not present at that energy level. (Level 1 has only s; Level 2 has s and p; Level 3 has s, p, and d.)

**Procedure:**
1. Place pennies (in order, lowest level first) to represent the electrons for atoms with atomic numbers Z = 3 (lithium), Z = 5 (boron), Z = 10 (neon), Z = 20 (calcium).
2. For each element, identify which level the outermost electron(s) are in. These are the **valence electrons**.
3. Record the number of valence electrons for each element.

**Analysis questions:**
1. Which of the four elements has the most valence electrons?
2. Neon (Z = 10) has a completely filled outer level. How does this relate to neon's chemical behavior (hint: noble gas)?
3. Lithium (Z = 3) and sodium (Z = 11) both have 1 valence electron. What does this suggest about their chemical properties?

**Cornell integration:** After the lab, students add one line to their Chunk B notes: "Valence electrons = electrons in the outermost [energy level]. Number of valence electrons determines [chemical properties]." The penny model makes the orbital filling rule concrete before the CER asks them to explain photon emission.

**Lab timing:** Fits between Chunks B and C (~20 min). Transition: "You've just shown where electrons live; now let's talk about what happens when they move between levels."
