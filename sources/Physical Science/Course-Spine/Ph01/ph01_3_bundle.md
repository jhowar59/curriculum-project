## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§ph01.3 introduces force as the cause of acceleration, connecting the kinematic vocabulary of §ph01.1–2 to Newton's laws. Four concept clusters:

1. **What is force** — force as a push or pull that can change an object's velocity (cause acceleration); contact forces vs. forces at a distance; balanced vs. unbalanced forces; net force determines whether acceleration occurs.
2. **Newton's 1st Law (inertia)** — objects at rest stay at rest; objects in motion stay in motion at constant velocity unless acted on by a net force; inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in motion; greater mass = greater inertia.
3. **Friction** — force opposing motion between surfaces in contact; static friction (surfaces not moving) > kinetic/sliding friction > rolling friction; friction depends on surface type and normal force; applications (car brakes, seatbelts).
4. **Air resistance** — a type of fluid friction; increases with speed; leads to terminal velocity when air resistance = weight (preview of §ph02.2).

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A hockey puck slides across an ice rink and slowly comes to a stop even though no one is pushing it. **Claim:** Identify the force or forces responsible for stopping the puck, and state which of Newton's laws explains what happens. **Evidence:** Use the types of friction from the reading to name the specific friction force acting on the puck (static, kinetic/sliding, or rolling?) and describe the direction it acts relative to the puck's motion. **Reasoning:** Explain why the puck would never stop if it were sliding on a perfectly frictionless surface — use Newton's 1st Law in your reasoning.
>
> *(Open own notes; ~4 sentences; graded on: correct friction type named, direction stated, Newton's 1st Law explicitly invoked, frictionless reasoning correct)*

**Scoring note:** Force = kinetic/sliding friction (puck is sliding, not rolling). Direction = opposite to motion. Newton's 1st Law: without a net force (friction), the puck would continue at constant velocity indefinitely (no change in motion). Common error: students say "friction pushes it backward" without specifying it is kinetic friction; accept "sliding friction" or "kinetic friction."

**Why this prompt works:** The sliding puck is the textbook Newton's 1st Law scenario. It requires students to apply force type vocabulary while reasoning from the law — not just restating it. The frictionless comparison makes the law concrete rather than abstract.

## 4 — In-class lab block (Friction Investigation)

**Lab title:** Static, Sliding, and Rolling Friction (embedded lab, §ph01.3)

**Materials per group:**
- Spring scale
- Wooden rods (4) or pencils (for rolling)
- Textbook (as load)
- String
- Scissors

**Procedure (abridged from textbook):**
1. Tie string around the textbook. Attach spring scale.
2. **Static friction:** Pull slowly until the book just starts to move. Record force at the moment of motion. Repeat 3×.
3. **Kinetic friction:** Pull at constant speed. Record force during steady sliding. Repeat 3×.
4. **Rolling friction:** Place book on 4 wooden rods. Pull at constant speed. Record force. Repeat 3×.
5. Calculate average for each type. Compare.

**Pre-lab hypothesis:** "Which type of friction is greatest? Which is smallest? Explain your prediction before testing."

**Debrief question:** How did your measurements compare to the textbook's claim (static > kinetic > rolling)? What happened to the spring scale reading when you changed from static to kinetic friction? How does this relate to why cars with anti-lock brakes stop faster than cars that skid?

**Cornell integration:** Record data table in notes. Add a sentence: "static > kinetic > rolling because ___." This is the evidence that supports the Ch11 §3 Key Idea about friction types.
