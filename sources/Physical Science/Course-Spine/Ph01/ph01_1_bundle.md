## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§ph01.1 builds the kinematic vocabulary the entire physics insert depends on. Three concept clusters:

1. **Motion and frame of reference** — an object is in motion when its position changes relative to a reference point; a frame of reference is the set of reference points used to describe that motion; direction matters.
2. **Distance vs. displacement** — distance measures total path length; displacement measures the straight-line change from start to end (includes direction). Displacement is a vector; distance is a scalar.
3. **Speed and velocity** — speed = distance / time; velocity = speed + direction (vector); combined velocities (bus + walker example); calculating average speed (v = d/t); instantaneous vs. average speed; graphing motion on d-t graphs (slope = speed, straight line = constant speed).

**QuickLab embedded — "Measuring Speed" (domino chain):** Students set up 25 dominoes, time the chain reaction for several spacings, and calculate average speed for each trial. Materials: 25 dominoes, meterstick, stopwatch. See lab block §4 below.

**Chunk breakdown (3 reading chunks, ~6–7 min each):**
- **Chunk A:** "Observing Motion" through "Displacement is the change of an object's position." Stop: after the soccer-field example with the black arrow. Cue: *A runner completes one lap around a 400 m track and returns to the start. What is their distance traveled? What is their displacement?*
- **Chunk B:** "Speed and Velocity" through "Calculating Speed" (including v = d/t and the sledder example calculation). Stop: after the average speed calculation for 132 m in 18 s. Cue: *A car travels 210 km in 3 hours. Calculate its average speed in km/h.*
- **Chunk C:** "Instantaneous speed" and "Graphing Motion" (d-t graphs, slope = speed). Stop: end of section. Cue: *On a distance-time graph, what does a steeper line mean? What does a horizontal line mean?*

**Day goal:** Establish that motion is relative, that displacement and distance are different, and that speed/velocity are quantitative. The d-t graph reading is the hardest idea here; spend debrief time on what slope means. Cornell + same-day CER; no quiz needed after §ph01.1 — acceleration in §ph01.2 follows the next day.

## 2 — Cue-column questions (Cornell left column)

*Students write the Q before reading the chunk; answer during the stop; summarize at the bottom.*

### Chunk A — Motion and Displacement
1. What is a frame of reference? Why do you need one to describe motion?
2. What is the difference between *distance* and *displacement*? Which one has both size and direction?
3. A student runs the perimeter of a rectangular field (100 m × 60 m) and stops at the starting point. What is the distance? What is the displacement?

### Chunk B — Speed and Velocity
4. What is the formula for average speed? What are the SI units?
5. Why is velocity different from speed? Give an example where you need velocity, not just speed.
6. A car travels 210 km in 3 hours. What is its average speed in km/h? Show the equation.
7. What is instantaneous speed? How does it differ from average speed?

### Chunk C — Graphing Motion
8. On a distance-time graph, what does the slope of the line represent?
9. A straight line on a d-t graph means the object is moving at _____ speed.
10. What does a horizontal line (slope = 0) mean on a d-t graph? What does it mean physically?

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A cyclist rides 600 m north, then turns around and rides 400 m south, finishing 200 m north of where she started. The whole trip takes 100 seconds. **Claim:** State (a) the total distance traveled and (b) the cyclist's displacement, including direction. **Evidence:** Show the calculation for the cyclist's average speed for the whole trip. **Reasoning:** Explain in one sentence why average speed and displacement give you different information — what does each one tell you that the other doesn't?
>
> *(Open own notes; ~4 sentences + one calculation; graded on: both (a) and (b) correct with direction stated, speed calculation set up correctly, reasoning sentence distinguishes scalar vs. vector)*

**Scoring note:** Distance = 1,000 m; displacement = 200 m north; average speed = 1,000 m ÷ 100 s = 10 m/s. Reasoning should note that distance tracks total path (useful for energy/fuel questions) while displacement tracks net change in position (useful for knowing where you ended up). Common error: students use displacement (200 m) in the speed formula instead of distance — address at debrief.

**Why this prompt works:** The round-trip scenario is the canonical distance-vs-displacement distinguisher. It requires calculating average speed from total distance (not displacement), which forces students to use the formula correctly. The reasoning question builds the scalar/vector vocabulary they need for HS-PS2-1 and HS-PS2-2 later.

## 4 — In-class lab block (Domino Speed — QuickLab)

**Lab title:** Measuring Speed (embedded QuickLab, §ph01.1)

**Materials per pair:**
- 25 dominoes
- Meterstick
- Stopwatch

**Procedure:**
1. Stand 25 dominoes in a straight line with equal spacing. Measure total length of the row in meters; record.
2. Tip the first domino. Time how long it takes all dominoes to fall; record.
3. Repeat 3 times using different spacings (smaller and larger distances between dominoes).
4. For each trial, calculate average speed = total length ÷ time.

**Debrief question:** How did spacing between dominoes change the average speed? Why? Relate your answer to the v = d/t formula — if distance (d) stays the same, what must change when speed increases?

**Cornell integration:** Students record trial data (spacing, time, speed) in a table in their notes. Sketch a d-t graph for one trial and label slope = speed. This is the first hands-on v = d/t use before the CER.

**Timing:** ~10 min total; run between Chunk B and Chunk C, or after Chunk C before CER.
