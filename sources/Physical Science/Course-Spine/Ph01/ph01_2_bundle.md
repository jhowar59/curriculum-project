## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§ph01.2 extends velocity into acceleration — the bridge concept between §ph01.1's kinematics and §ph02.1's Newton's 2nd Law. Three concept clusters:

1. **What is acceleration** — acceleration is any change in velocity (speed OR direction), not just speeding up; formula: a = Δv / Δt = (v_f − v_i) / t; SI unit m/s².
2. **Types of acceleration** — positive (speeding up in positive direction), negative/deceleration (slowing down), centripetal (changing direction, same speed); all three are accelerations. The "acceleration and motion" sidebar works through roller-coaster and car examples.
3. **Graphing accelerated motion** — on a velocity-time graph (v-t graph), the slope = acceleration; a straight line = constant acceleration; a horizontal line = constant velocity (zero acceleration); the area under a v-t curve = distance traveled.

**Free fall is treated briefly at the end:** all objects fall at the same rate near Earth (g = 9.8 m/s² downward) regardless of mass — preview of §ph02.2 gravity.

**Chunk breakdown (3 reading chunks, ~6–7 min each):**
- **Chunk A:** "What is Acceleration?" through the acceleration formula and unit; includes the examples of turning a corner and slowing down. Stop: after the calculation "a = (30 m/s − 0 m/s) / 5 s = 6 m/s²." Cue: *A car goes from 0 to 60 m/s in 10 s. Calculate its acceleration.*
- **Chunk B:** "Acceleration and Motion" — roller coaster, car turning, centripetal acceleration. Stop: after the section explains that changing direction is also acceleration. Cue: *A satellite moves in a circle at constant speed. Is it accelerating? Why or why not?*
- **Chunk C:** "Graphing Accelerated Motion" — v-t graphs, slope = acceleration, area = distance. Stop: end of section (free fall preview). Cue: *On a v-t graph, what does slope tell you? What does a horizontal line tell you?*

**Day goal:** Students should leave able to calculate acceleration from v_f, v_i, and t, and read slope from a v-t graph. The satellite question is a productive struggle — most students say "no" until they reason through velocity-as-vector.

## 2 — Cue-column questions (Cornell left column)

### Chunk A — Calculating Acceleration
1. Write the formula for acceleration. What are the units?
2. What three ways can an object accelerate? (Hint: velocity is a vector.)
3. A car goes from rest (0 m/s) to 30 m/s in 5 seconds. Calculate its acceleration. Show the formula.
4. If a car decelerates from 20 m/s to 0 m/s in 4 s, what is its acceleration? Is the value positive or negative?

### Chunk B — Types of Acceleration
5. Is turning a corner at constant speed considered accelerating? Explain why, using the definition of velocity.
6. A satellite orbits Earth at constant speed in a circle. Is it accelerating? What type of acceleration is this called?
7. Describe the motion of an object that is decelerating. How is deceleration still a type of acceleration?

### Chunk C — Graphing Accelerated Motion
8. On a velocity-time graph, what does the slope of the line represent?
9. What does a horizontal line on a v-t graph tell you about the object's motion?
10. A v-t graph shows a line going from 0 m/s at t=0 to 20 m/s at t=4 s. What is the object's acceleration? Show the slope calculation.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A school bus starts from rest at a stop and reaches a velocity of 18 m/s in 6 seconds. It then travels at constant velocity for 30 seconds. Finally, the brakes bring it to rest in 9 seconds. **Claim:** State whether the bus is accelerating, at constant velocity, or decelerating during each of the three phases. **Evidence:** Calculate the acceleration for Phase 1 and Phase 3 (show the formula for each). **Reasoning:** Explain what a value of zero acceleration means physically — what is the bus doing during Phase 2, and why does your v-t graph confirm this?
>
> *(Open own notes; 3 calculations + 2 reasoning sentences; graded on: all three phases correctly labeled, both calculations correct with units, Phase 2 explanation references constant velocity / zero Δv)*

**Scoring note:** Phase 1: a = 18/6 = 3 m/s² (positive). Phase 2: a = 0 (constant velocity). Phase 3: a = (0 − 18)/9 = −2 m/s² (negative/deceleration). For the v-t graph: Phase 1 is an upward-sloping line; Phase 2 is horizontal; Phase 3 is a downward-sloping line. Common error: students write "the bus is not moving" for Phase 2 rather than "moving at constant velocity" — redirect with: constant velocity ≠ no motion.

**Why this prompt works:** The three-phase scenario gives a complete kinematic picture and requires both calculation and graphical reasoning in a single context. It directly prepares students for the HS-PS2-1 data-analysis tasks where they interpret v-t or position-time data to support claims about net force.
