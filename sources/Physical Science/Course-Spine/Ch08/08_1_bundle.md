## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§8.1 builds the classification vocabulary for all of Ch8: pure substances vs. mixtures, heterogeneous vs. homogeneous, and the four heterogeneous mixture types (suspension, colloid, emulsion) before arriving at solutions (homogeneous mixtures) and the solute/solvent distinction.

1. **Heterogeneous vs. homogeneous** — suspensions (particles large enough to settle out or be filtered; orange juice example), colloids (particles intermediate in size, do not settle, scatter light — Tyndall effect), emulsions (liquid-in-liquid colloid with emulsifier; mayonnaise example).
2. **Homogeneous mixtures = solutions** — uniform at the particle level; solute separates into atoms, ions, or molecules when dissolved; solvent is what does the dissolving.
3. **Alloys as solid solutions** — brief extension; aquarium salt / NaCl in water as the canonical solute/solvent example.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Orange juice with pulp, milk, and salt water are all liquids that look similar at a glance. Using your notes on mixture types, **classify** each one (suspension, colloid, or solution) and **explain** your reasoning. For the suspension, describe what happens at the particle level that makes the particles eventually separate — and why that does NOT happen in a solution like salt water.
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: all three classified correctly with justification, particle-level explanation given for settling vs. not settling)*

**Scoring note:** Orange juice = suspension (pulp settles, can be filtered); milk = colloid (does not settle, scatters light); salt water = solution (ions uniformly distributed, too small to settle or scatter). Particle-level answer for settling: suspension particles are large (~1,000 nm) and gravity pulls them down; solution particles (ions/molecules) are far too small and are surrounded by solvent molecules that keep them distributed. On paper: quick holistic grade — award credit for classification + one particle-level detail. In Forms [short-answer] mode: Brisk grades on the three-part classification rubric.

**Why this prompt works:** It applies all three heterogeneous types and the solution definition in a single familiar scenario. The particle-level follow-up for the suspension previews §8.2's intermolecular-forces framing without requiring polarity knowledge yet.
