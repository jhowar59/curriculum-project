## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§8.2 is the PE core of Chapter 8. It delivers the complete HS-PS1-3 reasoning chain: water's polar structure → hydrogen bonding → ability to dissolve polar and ionic substances → "like dissolves like" as the predictive rule. It then extends to the dissolving process mechanics (rate factors) and colligative properties.

**OCR note:** Section clip opens at "Water dissolves many molecular compounds." — the §8.2 introductory paragraph(s) establishing water as the "universal solvent" are missing. Cover this briefly in teacher talk before Chunk A (≈1 min): "Water is sometimes called the universal solvent because it dissolves more substances than any other common liquid — but it does not dissolve everything."

1. **Water as polar solvent** — hydrogen bonding between water molecules; water dissolves polar molecular compounds (ethanol, sucrose) via H-bonding with –OH groups; water dissolves ionic compounds by pulling apart ions (Ch6 review); nonpolar compounds do not dissolve in water ("like dissolves like").
2. **The dissolving process** — kinetic theory drives dissolution; three rate factors: surface area (smaller pieces = more collisions), stirring (moves dissolved particles away from crystal surface), temperature (faster particles = more frequent/energetic collisions).
3. **Colligative properties** — solutes raise boiling point and lower freezing point of the solvent; amount of change depends on number of solute particles, not their identity (antifreeze / saltwater freezing-point examples).

**Chunk breakdown (3 reading chunks, ~6–8 min each):**
- **Chunk A:** Water's polarity → H-bonding → dissolving polar and ionic compounds. Stop point: after the "Like dissolves like" sub-heading and the nonpolar substances paragraph (before the Inquiry Lab). Cue: *Why does water dissolve NaCl but not olive oil?*
- **Chunk B:** Inquiry Lab + "The Dissolving Process" → rate factors: surface area, stirring, temperature. Stop point: after "Figure 8 shows the three ways to make solids dissolve faster." Cue: *Name three ways to make a solid dissolve faster. For each, explain WHY it works.*
- **Chunk C:** Solutes affect physical properties → colligative properties (boiling point elevation, freezing point depression). Stop point: end of section / Section 2 Review. Cue: *Why does adding salt to water lower its freezing point? What does this tell us about how solute particles interact with the solvent?*

## 2 — Cue-column questions (Cornell left column)

*Students write the Q before reading the chunk; answer during the stop; summarize at the bottom.*

### Chunk A — Polarity and Like Dissolves Like
1. What does it mean that water is a *polar* molecule? Which end is partially negative? Which is partially positive?
2. What is a hydrogen bond? How does it help water dissolve sucrose?
3. What is the rule "like dissolves like"? What does it predict about olive oil in water?
4. Why do nonpolar molecules NOT dissolve in water? (Think about the forces involved.)

### Chunk B — Rate Factors (Dissolving Process)
5. What does the kinetic theory of matter say that explains why anything dissolves at all?
6. Why does grinding a solid into smaller pieces help it dissolve faster? (Use the word *surface area*.)
7. Why does stirring speed up dissolving? What is happening at the crystal surface when you DON'T stir?
8. Why does heating the solvent speed up dissolving? Give two reasons.

### Chunk C — Colligative Properties
9. What happens to the boiling point and freezing point of water when you dissolve something in it?
10. Does it matter *what* you dissolve, or only *how much* you dissolve? Explain.
11. What is antifreeze, and why does it work?

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A lab technician needs to dissolve two substances: sodium chloride, NaCl (an ionic compound), and vegetable oil (a nonpolar substance). She has two solvents available: water (polar) and hexane, a nonpolar solvent derived from petroleum.
>
> **Predict** which solvent will dissolve each substance. Then **explain** your reasoning using the molecular structure of water and the "like dissolves like" rule. Your explanation should connect the *type of electrical forces* between solvent and solute molecules to whether dissolving occurs.
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: both predictions correct, "like dissolves like" rule cited, molecular-level explanation given connecting polarity/charge to attraction)*

**Scoring note:** NaCl (ionic) → water (polar) dissolves it because water's partial charges attract the Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions; hexane (nonpolar) cannot. Vegetable oil (nonpolar) → hexane dissolves it because nonpolar molecules attract other nonpolar molecules; water cannot because the attraction between water and oil is weaker than oil-oil attraction. Full credit requires the mechanism (electrical forces / partial charges / polarity), not just the rule. In Forms [short-answer] mode: Brisk grades on three-part rubric (both predictions, rule cited, mechanism stated). On paper: scan for polarity vocabulary and both predictions.

**Why this prompt works:** This is the priority CER bolt-on from the REPORT_CARD. It requires applying HS-PS1-3's exact reasoning chain — molecular structure → intermolecular electrical forces → macroscopic solubility — to a novel case. Students who have only memorized "like dissolves like" without understanding *why* will miss the mechanism half. Good notes from Chunk A provide everything needed.

## 4 — In-class lab block (§8.2 day, ~15–20 min)

**Inquiry Lab — What Will Dissolve a Nonpolar Substance? (p. 269, embedded in textbook)**

*This lab is printed in the textbook clip (between Chunk A and Chunk B). It directly satisfies HS-PS1-3 SEP-3 (plan and conduct an investigation).*

**Materials:** cotton swabs (3), tincture of iodine, water, ethanol (70% isopropyl or ethanol); optionally extend with rubbing alcohol, NaCl, food coloring, vegetable oil, test tubes.

**Procedure (as printed in textbook):**
1. Make two small iodine spots on palm of hand; let dry.
2. Wash one spot with water (cotton swab). Observe.
3. Wash other spot with ethanol (cotton swab). Observe.

**Analysis questions (from textbook):**
- Did water or ethanol dissolve the iodine spot better?
- Is water more polar or less polar than ethanol? Explain your reasoning.

**Cornell integration:** Students record observations in their notes table (Chunk B stop point), then write a 2-sentence conclusion: "Iodine is a [polar/nonpolar] substance because ___. This explains why ___ dissolved it and ___ did not."

**Extension (if materials available):** Add test tubes; test NaCl + food coloring in water vs. rubbing alcohol; test vegetable oil in water vs. rubbing alcohol. This converts the QuickLab into the full "like-dissolves-like" investigation flagged in the REPORT_CARD as the chapter's highest-priority lab gap. Equipment: test tubes, water, 70% isopropyl alcohol, NaCl, food coloring, vegetable oil — all likely in §3 solvents inventory.

**Safety:** Iodine stains — work over paper towels. Ethanol/isopropyl: no open flames.
