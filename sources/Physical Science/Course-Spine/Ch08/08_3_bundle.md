## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§8.3 is the quantitative extension of §8.2's solubility explanation. It moves from *why* substances dissolve (§8.2) to *how much* they dissolve and *how to measure it*.

**OCR note:** Section clip opens at "Different substances have different solubilities." — the §8.3 introductory paragraph is missing. Cover in teacher talk (≈1 min): "Section 8.3 asks: once we know a substance CAN dissolve in water, how much of it will dissolve? The answer is called solubility — and it depends on the same intermolecular forces we studied in §8.2."

1. **Solubility** — maximum grams of solute per 100 g of solvent at a given temperature and pressure; varies by substance because it depends on relative strengths of solute-solute forces vs. solvent-solute forces (sodium iodide vs. sodium chloride vs. sodium fluoride comparison table); solubility of solids increases with temperature; solubility of gases decreases with temperature and increases with pressure (soda/CO₂ example; bends in scuba divers).
2. **Saturated, unsaturated, supersaturated** — unsaturated = can dissolve more; saturated = at the maximum; supersaturated = holds more than the equilibrium amount (unstable; crystallizes on seeding). Sodium acetate hand-warmer example.
3. **Concentration and molarity** — qualitative descriptors (dilute/concentrated, weak/strong) are imprecise; molarity = moles of solute per liter of solution (M = mol/L); worked example (sucrose solution); math skills box with two practice problems.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> At 20 °C, the solubility of sodium chloride (NaCl) is 35.9 g per 100 g of water (from Figure 2 in your textbook).
>
> **Part A — Saturation:** You add 50 g of NaCl to 100 g of water at 20 °C and stir thoroughly. Describe what the resulting solution looks like and classify it (unsaturated, saturated, or supersaturated). Explain what is happening at the particle level with the undissolved solid at the bottom.
>
> **Part B — Molarity:** A different NaCl solution contains 0.500 mol of NaCl dissolved in 2.00 L of solution. Calculate the molarity. Show your work and include units.
>
> *(Part A: 2–3 sentences + classification; Part B: calculation with work shown; open own notes; graded on: correct classification with particle-level reasoning, correct molarity with units)*

**Scoring note:** Part A: saturated (only 35.9 g dissolves; 50 − 35.9 = 14.1 g remains undissolved); at the particle level, the saturated solution is at equilibrium — ions dissolve and re-precipitate at the same rate, so the solid does not continue to dissolve. Part B: M = 0.500 mol ÷ 2.00 L = 0.250 M. Award full credit for correct setup, correct answer, and units. Partial credit for correct formula setup with arithmetic error. In Forms [short-answer] mode: Brisk grades Part A on classification + particle reasoning; Part B can be partially auto-graded by answer-matching "0.250 M."

**Why this prompt works:** Part A applies the saturation concept and connects it back to §8.2's particle-level language — students must use "equilibrium" or "dissolve/settle" language from their notes. Part B is the first molarity calculation item in the chapter (the REPORT_CARD flags this as a gap — the CFU and test both fail to practice the calculation). Together, the two parts cover both the conceptual and quantitative sides of §8.3 in a single open-notes item.
