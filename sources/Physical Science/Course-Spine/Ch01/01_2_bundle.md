## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§1.2 translates "how science works" from the Roentgen narrative into the working tools scientists actually use. Two main concept groups:

1. **Scientific Methods** — what a hypothesis is and how it guides an experiment; the difference between independent and dependent variables; why *no* experiment is truly a failure (unexpected results drive new questions — a direct callback to Roentgen's reaction in §1.1).
2. **Measurement + SI Units** — quantitative vs. qualitative observation; the SI system (base units: s, m, kg, m³ + common derived prefixes); measuring time, length, mass, and volume with appropriate tools; mass ≠ weight (critical misconception catch).

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A student has 50 mL of water in a graduated cylinder. She drops a small metal cube into the cylinder and the water level rises to 62 mL. She wants to find the **mass** of the cube, so she puts it on a balance and reads 96.3 g.
>
> **Claim:** Did the student measure the right thing to find the cube's mass? Was the graduated-cylinder step necessary for finding mass?
>
> **Explain** your reasoning using the definitions of *mass* and *volume* from your notes. Then describe what the graduated-cylinder measurement actually does tell her about the cube.
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: claim stated, definitions used correctly, volume/mass distinction explained)*

**Scoring note:** The claim answer is: the balance gives mass correctly (no, the cylinder step was not needed for mass). The cylinder step gives *volume* of the cube (by water displacement). Both terms must appear correctly defined in the student's notes to write a graded answer — this is what "open *own* notes" enforces. Thin or copied notes cannot supply correct definitions.

**Why this prompt works:** It applies both quantities from Chunk B in a single scenario, forces the mass/weight (actually mass/volume here) distinction without telegraphing it, and rewards a student who understood the graduated-cylinder figure in the textbook.
