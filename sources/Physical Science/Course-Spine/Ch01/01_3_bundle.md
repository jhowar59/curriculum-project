## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§1.3 is the most math-intensive section of Ch1. It sits entirely in the SEP "analyze and interpret data / use mathematics" cluster. Three concept groups:

1. **Presenting Data** — why organizing data matters; three graph types and when to use each: *line graph* (continuous change over time, independent variable on x-axis), *bar graph* (comparing individual values), *pie graph* (parts of a whole). The Roentgen callback appears in the opener (reading others' data helped him make his discovery — communication matters).
2. **Scientific Notation** — expressing very large/small quantities as a coefficient × 10^n; calculating (multiply → add exponents, divide → subtract exponents). Worked examples use speed of light and Earth–Neptune distance.
3. **Significant Figures** — *precision* = exactness (number of sig figs); *accuracy* = closeness to the true value; these are different. Rounding rules for multiplication/division (match fewest sig figs) vs. addition/subtraction (match fewest decimal places).

## 2 — Cue-column questions (Cornell left column)

*Students write the Q before reading the chunk; add answer during the stop; summarize at the bottom.*

### Chunk A — Presenting Scientific Data
1. Why is organizing and presenting data an important science skill?
2. What type of graph should you use for data that change *continuously* over time? Where does the independent variable go?
3. What is the difference between a bar graph and a pie graph? When would you use each?

### Chunk B — Scientific Notation
4. What is scientific notation, and why do scientists use it?
5. Write the rule for **multiplying** two numbers in scientific notation. Write the rule for **dividing**.
6. Convert: 300,000,000 m/s to scientific notation. Then divide it into 4,500,000,000,000 m. What is the result?

### Chunk C — Significant Figures
7. What does the *number of significant figures* in a measurement tell you about that measurement?
8. What is the difference between *precision* and *accuracy*? Can a measurement be precise but not accurate? Give an example.
9. When you **multiply** two measurements, how do you decide how many significant figures to keep in your answer?

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A student measures a rectangular piece of aluminum foil for a density experiment. She records: length = 15.4 cm, width = 8.67 cm. She calculates area on her calculator and gets: **133.518 cm²**.
>
> **Part A — Significant figures:** How many significant figures should the answer have? Write the correctly rounded area. Use your notes to justify your rounding rule.
>
> **Part B — Graph choice:** After repeating the measurement on 10 different pieces of foil, she wants to compare the area of each piece. What type of graph should she use to display this comparison? What type of graph would she use if she wanted to show how the area *changed* over time as she cut the foil into smaller pieces? Explain the difference using vocabulary from your notes.
>
> *(3–5 sentences per part; open own notes; graded on: correct sig fig answer with rule cited, correct graph types named and distinguished)*

**Scoring note:** Part A: 3 sig figs (limited by 15.4); correctly rounded = **134 cm²**. Students who can't cite the rule in their notes will guess. Part B: bar graph for comparing individual items; line graph for showing continuous change over time. Both answers must come from the student's own definitions — open-notes only pays off if the definitions are there.

**Why this prompt works:** It applies §1.3 content immediately (sig figs + graph choice) in a single scenario, and each part tests one concept cleanly. The sig-fig calculation is non-trivial enough that guessing the rule produces wrong answers, but derivable from clear notes.

## 4 — In-class lab block (§1.3 day, ~15–20 min)

**QuickLab — Precision vs. Accuracy (p. 27, from the textbook)**
- Materials: 5 crumpled paper balls per student, 1 trash bin
- Round 1: bin 2 m away — throw all 5; record how many went in and where they landed
- Round 2: move bin 2 m farther (4 m total) — throw again
- Debrief: *Were your throws precise? Accurate? What's the difference?*
- Cornell note integration: students write "precision = ___; accuracy = ___" from their Chunk C reading, then use these definitions to describe their throws in 2 sentences (this IS the note they'll use on the CER for Part B)

**Optional extension — Water Displacement Lab (ties §1.2 to §1.3)**
- Materials: 100 mL graduated cylinder, small rock or dense object, tap water
- Procedure: measure initial water volume; add object; record new volume; calculate object's volume by subtraction
- Record in a data table (3 trials, average)
- Cornell integration: table goes directly into the notes page — students have a real data table they made
- Connection: mass/weight from §1.2; volume from §1.2; data tables from §1.3 Chunk A

## 5 — Bell-ringer CFU (NEXT DAY, closed notes)

*Use the first day of Ch2 to close the loop on §1.3.*

- A. Convert 0.0000021 m to scientific notation. Show your work.
- B. A student calculates 8.871 m × 9.14 m = 81.08094 m² on her calculator. What is the correctly rounded answer? How many sig figs?
- C. (Harder) A student throws three darts. All three hit the board near the same spot — but that spot is far from the bull's-eye. Are the throws *precise*, *accurate*, both, or neither? Explain.
