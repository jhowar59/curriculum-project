## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§ph03.2 is the PS3-1 and PS3-2 primary delivery — the section where students first calculate PE and KE and model energy as associated with position and motion. Three concept clusters:

1. **Gravitational potential energy (PE = mgh)** — energy stored due to position; depends on mass and height; PE = mass × g × height; joules; height is relative to a reference point; connecting to §ph03.1: lifting something against gravity = doing work, and that work becomes stored PE.
2. **Elastic potential energy** — energy stored in stretched or compressed objects (rubber bands, springs); briefly introduced; not quantified in this section.
3. **Kinetic energy (KE = ½mv²)** — energy of motion; depends on mass and speed²; the speed-squared relationship means a small speed change has a large KE effect (car crash example); atoms/molecules have KE (connection to temperature and the thermal energy theme from earlier chemistry chapters — PS3-1 cross-cutting thread).

**Chunk breakdown (3 reading chunks, ~6–7 min each):**
- **Chunk A:** Gravitational PE (PE = mgh, worked example: 65 kg rock climber at 35 m). Stop: after the practice problems (PE of car, climber, bird). Cue: *A 2 kg book sits on a 1.5 m tall shelf. Calculate its gravitational PE. If the shelf were twice as high, how would the PE change?*
- **Chunk B:** Kinetic energy (KE = ½mv²; speed-squared relationship; snowboarder graph). Stop: after the car-crash example (speed doubled → KE quadruples). Cue: *A 1,000 kg car moves at 20 m/s. Calculate its KE. If the speed doubles to 40 m/s, what happens to KE? Show the calculation.*
- **Chunk C:** Atoms/molecules have KE (temperature connection) and brief elastic PE recap. Stop: end of section. Cue: *How is the kinetic energy of atoms connected to an object's temperature? What happens to atom KE when an object is heated?*

**Day goal:** Students can calculate PE = mgh and KE = ½mv² and know that PE depends on position (height) while KE depends on motion (speed). The speed-squared result is the key surprise — it explains why speeding in a car is disproportionately dangerous. The molecule-KE connection links physics to the thermal energy theme they've already seen in the chemistry chapters (HS-PS3-2's cross-phase connection).

## 2 — Cue-column questions (Cornell left column)

### Chunk A — Gravitational Potential Energy
1. What is potential energy? What kind of PE depends on height?
2. Write the formula for gravitational PE. What are the units? What does each variable represent?
3. A 65 kg climber is 35 m above the base of a cliff. Calculate the gravitational PE. (g = 9.8 m/s²)
4. If the climber descends to 10 m, calculate the new PE. How much PE was released?
5. Is PE "stored" in the climber or in the climber-Earth system? Explain.

### Chunk B — Kinetic Energy
6. What is kinetic energy? Write the formula. What are the units?
7. Why does KE depend on speed *squared* but only on mass to the *first power*?
8. A 1,000 kg car moves at 20 m/s. Calculate its KE.
9. The same car doubles its speed to 40 m/s. Calculate the new KE. By what factor did KE change?
10. Why is speeding "twice as dangerous" an understatement? What does the math actually show?

### Chunk C — Molecular KE and Temperature
11. Do atoms and molecules have kinetic energy? Why?
12. What is the relationship between the average kinetic energy of particles and the temperature of an object?
13. When you heat a gas, what happens to the average KE of the gas molecules? How does this connect to PS3-2 (energy associated with motion)?

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A 0.5 kg ball is lifted to a height of 4 m and then released. Just before it hits the ground, the ball is moving at 8.85 m/s. **Claim:** Identify the form of energy the ball has at the top (at 4 m height) and the form it has just before impact (at ground level). **Evidence:** Calculate the ball's gravitational PE at 4 m (PE = mgh) and the ball's KE just before impact (KE = ½mv²). Show both calculations. **Reasoning:** Compare the two values. Are they equal? Explain what would have to be true about energy for these values to be equal — and what this result suggests about what happened to the PE as the ball fell.
>
> *(Open own notes; 2 calculations + 2 reasoning sentences; graded on: PE and KE both calculated correctly, comparison made, energy transformation stated)*

**Scoring note:** PE = 0.5 × 9.8 × 4 = 19.6 J. KE = ½ × 0.5 × (8.85)² = ½ × 0.5 × 78.3 = 19.6 J. They are equal. Reasoning: all the PE at the top was converted to KE at the bottom — energy transformed from potential to kinetic (foreshadowing conservation of energy). Common error: students who get different values should check arithmetic; the prompt is designed so the values are equal. Students who reason "energy was conserved" are partially ahead of §ph03.3 — affirm their reasoning.

**Why this prompt works:** It bridges PE and KE in a single scenario and plants the seed for conservation of energy without stating it yet. Students who notice the values are equal are doing exactly what §ph03.3 formalizes. This is HS-PS3-1 (calculate change in energy) and HS-PS3-2 (model energy associated with position vs. motion) in one problem.
