## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§ph03.1 establishes work, power, and mechanical advantage as the mechanical-energy foundation. Three concept clusters:

1. **Work** — work is done when a force causes displacement in the direction of the force (W = F × d); SI unit = joule (J = N·m); no displacement = no work (carrying a book horizontally does NO work on it in the physics sense); work is a scalar.
2. **Power** — power is the rate of doing work (P = W/t); SI unit = watt (W = J/s); running upstairs vs. walking upstairs: same work, different power; the stair-climbing QuickLab makes this concrete.
3. **Machines and mechanical advantage** — a machine multiplies force or distance; mechanical advantage (MA) = output force / input force = input distance / output distance; MA > 1 means force is multiplied; simple machine examples (ramp, lever, pulley); machines don't create energy — they trade force for distance.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Two movers load a 400 N box into a truck. Mover A pushes it directly up a ramp that is 4 m long (height = 1 m) in 8 seconds. Mover B lifts the same box straight up 1 m in 4 seconds. **Claim:** State the work done by each mover. **Evidence:** Calculate (1) the work done by each mover (W = Fd, where d is the distance the force is applied and F is the force needed), and (2) the power of each mover (P = W/t). For Mover A, use F = W/MA (MA = 4 m / 1 m = 4). **Reasoning:** Explain the trade-off the ramp provides: what does it multiply, and what does it sacrifice? Is the ramp "saving" energy?
>
> *(Open own notes; 2 calculations per mover + 2 reasoning sentences; graded on: both work values equal (or proportionally compared), power values correct, trade-off explanation correct)*

**Scoring note:** Mover B: W = 400 N × 1 m = 400 J; P = 400/4 = 100 W. Mover A: MA = 4, so force = 400/4 = 100 N; W = 100 N × 4 m = 400 J; P = 400/8 = 50 W. Both do the same work (400 J). Ramp multiplies force (100 N vs. 400 N) but sacrifices distance (4 m vs. 1 m). No energy saved — just force reduced at the cost of distance. Common error: students say the ramp "does less work" — work is the same; only force per distance changes.

**Why this prompt works:** It directly applies W = Fd and mechanical advantage in the same scenario, showing the trade-off without reducing it to formula plugging. The "is the ramp saving energy?" reasoning question targets the key misconception (machines don't create energy — they redistribute it). This scaffolds PS3-1's energy-accounting lens for §ph03.2.

## 4 — In-class lab block (Power Output — QuickLab)

**Lab title:** Power Output (embedded QuickLab, §ph03.1)

**Materials:**
- Bathroom scale (or known weight calculation: mass × 9.8 N/kg)
- Stopwatch
- Meterstick
- Staircase (or equivalent)

**Procedure:**
1. Use the scale to find your weight in newtons (lb × 4.45 = N).
2. Measure the height of one stair step in meters. Count steps and calculate total staircase height.
3. Time a classmate walking up the stairs quickly; record time.
4. Calculate work done (W = weight × stair height).
5. Calculate power (P = W / time).
6. Repeat while carrying an extra 20 N load.

**Analysis questions:**
1. How did carrying extra books change your power output? Why?
2. What would your power output be if you climbed twice as fast? (Don't recalculate — just reason from the formula.)
3. A 60 W light bulb uses 60 J per second. How does your stair-climbing power compare?

**Cornell integration:** Students record: weight (N), stair height (m), time (s), work done (J), power (W). Convert to horsepower if desired (1 hp ≈ 746 W). Write one sentence: "I did ___ J of work in ___ s for a power of ___ W — that's [more/less] than a 60 W light bulb."
