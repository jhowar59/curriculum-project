## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§ph03.3 completes the PS3-1 delivery by formalizing conservation of energy and introducing efficiency as the real-world modifier. Three concept clusters:

1. **Law of conservation of energy** — energy cannot be created or destroyed; only its form can change; total energy in a closed system is constant; roller-coaster example (conveyor belt → PE at top → KE at bottom → PE again → … → heat/sound at friction surfaces); the full cycle always sums to the same total.
2. **Energy transformations** — PE ↔ KE is the cleanest cycle; but also KE → thermal (friction), KE → sound (collision), chemical → KE (combustion engines); energy doesn't disappear — it transforms to a less useful form.
3. **Efficiency** — real machines always output less useful work than input work because some energy becomes heat via friction; efficiency = (useful work output / total work input) × 100%; an ideal machine has 100% efficiency; real machines do not; increasing efficiency means reducing friction losses.

**Lab embedded — "Energy of a Rolling Ball":** Students roll a ball down a ramp at three heights, record PE at top and calculate KE at the bottom from average speed, then compare PE_initial to KE_final. The difference (PE_initial − KE_final) represents energy lost to friction/rolling resistance. See lab block §4 below.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A 500 kg roller coaster car starts from rest at the top of a 40 m hill. At the bottom of the hill, engineers measure the car's speed to be 26 m/s. **Claim:** State whether energy was conserved in this scenario (use PE_initial vs. KE_final to decide). **Evidence:** Calculate PE at the top (PE = mgh) and KE at the bottom (KE = ½mv²). Show both calculations with units. Compare the two values. **Reasoning:** If PE ≠ KE, identify where the "missing" energy went. Calculate what percentage of the initial PE was converted to useful KE (this is the efficiency of this descent). What physical process caused the loss?
>
> *(Open own notes; 2 calculations + 2 reasoning sentences; graded on: both calculations correct, comparison made, energy destination identified as friction/heat, efficiency calculated)*

**Scoring note:** PE = 500 × 9.8 × 40 = 196,000 J. KE = ½ × 500 × 26² = ½ × 500 × 676 = 169,000 J. PE > KE by 27,000 J. Efficiency = 169,000/196,000 × 100% ≈ 86%. The remaining 14% (~27,000 J) was converted to thermal energy (friction between wheels and track, air resistance). This is PS3-1's energy accounting at work.

**Why this prompt works:** It delivers the full PS3-1 skill in one problem: calculate change in energy of one component (PE dropping to zero), identify the energy flow (PE → KE + heat), and quantify the fraction that is useful (efficiency). The roller coaster scenario is the same one the section introduces, making it directly anchored to the reading.

## 4 — In-class lab block (Energy of a Rolling Ball)

**Lab title:** Energy of a Rolling Ball (§ph03.3)

**Materials per group:**
- Wooden board (ramp) ~1–2 m long
- 3 different heights of books/stacks for the ramp
- Rubber or wooden ball
- Meterstick
- Stopwatch
- Masking tape (mark 1 m from end of ramp)

**Procedure:**
1. Build ramp at Height 1 (~10–15 cm). Measure height and ramp length. Record ball mass.
2. Release ball from top. Time travel from top to 1 m mark at the bottom. Repeat 3×; find average time.
3. Calculate average speed (v = d/t, using 1 m / average time) and estimate final speed (≈ 2 × average speed for uniform acceleration).
4. Calculate KE_final = ½mv².
5. Calculate PE_initial = mgh.
6. Find PE_initial − KE_final (energy "lost" to friction and rolling resistance).
7. Repeat for Height 2 and Height 3.

**Analysis questions:**
1. As height increased, what happened to PE_initial? What happened to KE_final?
2. Was PE_initial ever exactly equal to KE_final? What explains the difference?
3. At which height was the energy loss (PE − KE) greatest? Why might that be?

**Cornell integration:** Data table in notes (Height, PE, KE, PE−KE, Efficiency%). Students calculate efficiency % for each trial and write: "At height ___ m, efficiency = ___%. Energy loss was due to ___."
