## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
This clip contains **§5.3 Families of Elements** content. It is the application layer that closes the HS-PS1-1 arc: students now see the valence-electron → property rule play out across five named families, each with distinct behaviors. The section also introduces the QuickLab "Elements in Food."

Five families are covered, organized into metal and nonmetal groups:

1. **Metals — Three Families:**
   - *Alkali metals (Group 1):* 1 valence electron; very reactive; stored in oil; form 1+ cations (Na⁺, K⁺, Li⁺); not found as pure elements in nature.
   - *Alkaline-earth metals (Group 2):* 2 valence electrons; reactive but less so than alkali metals; form 2+ cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺); found in limestone, coral, bones, and teeth; Mg is the lightest structural metal.
   - *Transition metals (Groups 3–12):* varied valence electrons; generally less reactive than Groups 1/2; variable ion charges (e.g., Au⁺ and Au³⁺); good conductors; higher melting points.

2. **Nonmetals — Two Families:**
   - *Halogens (Group 17):* 7 valence electrons; most reactive nonmetals; gain 1 electron to form 1– anions; combine with alkali metals to form salts (NaCl, NaF, NaBr, NaI); real-world uses: chlorine in pool/drinking water, fluoride in toothpaste, iodized salt.
   - *Noble gases (Group 18):* 8 valence electrons (except He = 2); outer level already filled → unreactive; exist as individual atoms; used in lighting (neon signs, fluorescent bulbs).

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Two elements are in Group 17: **chlorine (Cl)** and **iodine (I)**. Both are halogens.
>
> **Using your notes, explain:** (1) How many valence electrons does each halogen have, and what ion does each form? (2) Predict what compound forms when chlorine reacts with sodium (Na). Write the compound formula and name it. (3) Noble gases (Group 18) are directly to the right of halogens on the periodic table. Explain in one sentence why noble gases are unreactive while halogens are extremely reactive — use valence electrons in your answer.
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: correct valence electron count for halogens, correct compound formula and name, correct noble gas explanation using "filled outer level" or equivalent language)*

**Scoring note:** (1) Both have 7 valence electrons; both form 1– ions (Cl⁻, I⁻). (2) NaCl — sodium chloride (table salt). (3) Noble gases already have 8 valence electrons (a filled outer level) so they do not need to gain or lose electrons; halogens have 7 — one short of filled — so they readily gain an electron. Paper version: scan for "7 valence electrons," "NaCl," and the filled/unfilled outer level contrast.

**Why this prompt works:** Part 3 requires students to compare two adjacent groups using the same valence-electron logic — which is the HS-PS1-1 synthesis the REPORT_CARD identifies as the chapter's highest-priority CER opportunity (CFU 5.3-C bolt-on). It cannot be answered from recall alone if the student didn't read; it can be answered efficiently if the Cornell notes from Chunks B and C captured the key patterns.

## 4 — In-class lab block (QuickLab: Elements in Food, ~20 min or take-home)

**QuickLab — Elements in Food** (from §5.3, textbook p. ~155)

*This is a structured observation/research task, not a wet lab.*

- **Step 1:** For 1 day (assign as take-home or give 5 min in class to start with a food wrapper on hand), list all ingredients from foods and drinks consumed.
- **Step 2:** Identify which items on the list are *compounds* (multiple elements chemically combined) vs. pure elements or mixtures.
- **Step 3:** For each compound, identify which elements make it up (e.g., NaCl → sodium + chlorine; Ca compounds in milk → calcium + others).
- **Cornell integration:** Students add a section at the bottom of their §5.3 notes page: a 2-column table with "Compound found in food" and "Element families represented." At least one alkali metal (Na), one alkaline-earth metal (Ca), and one halogen (Cl, F in fluoride) should appear in most food logs.
- **Debrief question (oral or in notes):** *Which element families showed up most often in your food? Why does that make sense given what you know about alkali metals and alkaline-earth metals being found in living things?*

**Equipment:** None — paper and food packaging. Works as a take-home assignment started in class.

## 5 — Bell-ringer CFU (NEXT DAY, closed notes)

*Use the start of the next class period (or first day of Ch6) as a closed-notes recall check.*

- A. Name the five element families covered in §5.3. For each, give its group number and number of valence electrons.
- B. Explain why halogens are the most reactive nonmetals and noble gases are the least reactive. Use "valence electrons" in your answer.
- C. (Harder) Potassium (K, Group 1) and bromine (Br, Group 17) react with each other. Predict the formula of the compound they form and explain why each element reacts the way it does. *(Answer: KBr — potassium loses its 1 valence electron to form K⁺; bromine gains 1 electron to fill its outer level and form Br⁻; the ions attract.)*

*In Forms mode: A is [auto-grade] if table-matching MCQ; B and C are [short-answer]. On paper: C is the richest check — it requires both families, both ion directions, and the attraction logic that previews Ch6 ionic bonding.*
