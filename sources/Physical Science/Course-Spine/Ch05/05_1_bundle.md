## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
This clip contains **§5.2 Exploring the Periodic Table** content. It is the valence-electron payoff section of the chapter: why elements in a group share properties, how electrons fill energy levels, how ionization produces charged atoms, and the three broad element categories (metals, nonmetals, semiconductors). This is the heart of HS-PS1-1.

> **OCR / sequence note:** §5.1 (Organizing the Elements — Mendeleev's history, Moseley's atomic-number revision, the periodic law, periods and groups as structural vocabulary) is **absent from the OCR**. The clip `05_1.md` opens with §5.2 content. **Before assigning this reading, teachers should briefly review the layout of the periodic table (atomic number, periods, groups) using the Ch5 deck's opening slides (sub-deck 5.1-A), or walk through it verbally (~5 min). Students need the vocabulary "period," "group," and "atomic number" before Chunk A makes sense.**

Three concept clusters, each a natural reading stop:

1. **The Role of Electrons / Valence Electrons** — atoms in the same group have the same number of valence electrons; that sameness explains similar chemical properties. Period placement maps to which energy level is being filled.
2. **Ion Formation** — atoms whose outer orbitals aren't full may gain or lose electrons; gaining electrons → negative ion (anion); losing electrons → positive ion (cation). Group 1 easily loses one electron; Group 17 easily gains one.
3. **How Are Elements Classified?** — three categories: metals (majority; shiny, ductile, conductive), nonmetals (right side of PT; poor conductors, may be solid/liquid/gas), semiconductors (six elements including Si and Ge; conduct electricity only under some conditions; basis for computer chips).

**Chunk breakdown (3 reading chunks, ~6–8 min each):**
- **Chunk A:** "The Role of Electrons" through "An element's location in the periodic table is related to electron arrangement." Stop: after the carbon electron-arrangement table. Cue: *How many valence electrons does a lithium atom have? Why does that make it similar to sodium?*
- **Chunk B:** "Ion Formation" through "Reading Check Why do Group 1 and Group 17 elements easily form ions?" Stop: after the Reading Check question. Cue: *What is an ion? Explain how lithium forms Li⁺ and how fluorine forms F⁻.*
- **Chunk C:** "How Are Elements Classified?" through end of section. Stop: end of section. Cue: *Name the three main categories of elements and give one property that distinguishes each.*

**Day goal:** Chunk A establishes why the PT is a predictive tool (valence electrons → group behavior). Chunk B applies it to ion formation — the first time students derive an ion charge from position. Chunk C extends classification to three categories. The open-notes CER closes the period by asking students to predict behavior for a new element using only PT position.

## 2 — Cue-column questions (Cornell left column)

### Chunk A — The Role of Electrons / Valence Electrons
1. Why do elements in the same group of the periodic table have similar chemical properties?

3. How does the period an element is in tell you which energy level its valence electrons occupy?

### Chunk B — Ion Formation
4. What is an ion? How does an atom become an ion?
5. Why does a lithium atom (Group 1) lose an electron rather than gain seven electrons to become stable?

### Chunk C — Three Categories of Elements
7. What are the three main categories of elements on the periodic table?
8. List two properties of metals that distinguish them from nonmetals.
9. What makes a semiconductor different from a metal and a nonmetal? Give one example.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Cesium (Cs) is in Group 1, Period 6 of the periodic table. Potassium (K) is also in Group 1, but in Period 4.
>
> **Using your notes, explain:** (1) How many valence electrons does each element have? (2) Predict whether cesium forms a positive ion or a negative ion, and write the ion symbol. (3) Based on their group membership, would you classify both elements as metals, nonmetals, or semiconductors?
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: correct valence electron count stated, correct ion symbol with reasoning from notes, correct category with evidence)*

**Scoring note:** (1) Both have 1 valence electron — Group 1 rule. (2) Cs⁺ — Group 1 atoms lose their single valence electron. (3) Both are metals — Group 1 alkali metals are on the left side of the PT. Students who can't cite valence electron count from Group number are missing the core Chunk A idea. Paper version: scan for "1 valence electron," "Cs⁺," and "metal."

**Why this prompt works:** It requires students to transfer the Group 1 rule to an element (Cs) they almost certainly haven't memorized. Correct answers are only possible by applying the valence-electron logic from their notes — not by recall. This is HS-PS1-1 at application level on the first day it's introduced.

## 4 — Bell-ringer CFU (NEXT DAY, closed notes)

*Use the start of the next class period as a quick closed-notes recall check.*

- A. Elements in the same **group** of the periodic table have similar chemical properties. Why?
- B. Fluorine (Group 17) and sodium (Group 1) are both very reactive. Explain why each is reactive using valence electrons.
- C. (Harder) Magnesium is in Group 2. Predict: does it form Mg⁺ or Mg²⁺ ions? Explain your reasoning using the concept of a "filled outer energy level."

*In Forms mode: A is [auto-grade] if MCQ; B and C are [short-answer]. On paper: quick holistic grade — B: F needs 1 more electron (7 valence e⁻); Na loses 1 easily (1 valence e⁻). C: Mg²⁺ — Group 2 has 2 valence electrons, both lost to reach a filled outer level.*
