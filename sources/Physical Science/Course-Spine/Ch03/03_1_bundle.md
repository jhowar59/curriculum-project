## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**  
§3.1 builds the conceptual foundation for the entire chapter: what matter is made of, how particle motion explains the states of matter, and how temperature and thermal energy differ. Three main concept groups:

1. **Kinetic Theory** — matter is made of constantly-moving atoms/molecules; faster particles = higher temperature; more massive particles move slower at the same temperature. The kitchen aroma scenario opens it concretely.
2. **States of Matter** — solid (definite shape and volume, particles vibrate in place), liquid (definite volume, variable shape, particles slide past each other), gas (variable shape and volume, particles move fast enough to overcome all attraction), plasma (ionized particles, the most common state in the universe). The shape/volume classification grid is the key organizing table.
3. **Energy's Role** — energy is the capacity to do work; all moving particles have kinetic energy. Temperature = average KE of particles in an object. Thermal energy = total KE of all particles. The critical distinction: a large cool object can have more thermal energy than a small hot one because total particle count matters.

## 4 — In-class lab block (§3.1 day, ~10 min)

**QuickLab — Hot or Cold? (p. 81, from textbook)**

- Materials: three buckets — one warm, one cold, one hot (test hot water first; must be safe to put hands in)
- Procedure: place both hands in warm water; then one hand in cold, one in hot; after 1 minute move both back to warm water
- Record observations at each step
- Debrief question: *Can you rely on your hands to determine temperature accurately? What does this tell you about the difference between sensory perception and a scientific measure of temperature?*

**Cornell note integration:** After the debrief, students write one sentence in their notes: "Temperature is _____ because our senses only tell us ___." This sentence becomes the anchor for the Chunk C CER.

**Timing note:** Run after Chunk C (after reading the thermal energy distinction). The lab's point — that the same water feels different depending on what your hand was in before — makes the "average KE of particles" definition concrete. It also sets up the boiling lab in §3.2 as the quantitative version of the same question.
