## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**  
§3.3 is the most math-intensive section of the chapter. It covers the physics of fluids: pressure, buoyancy, Pascal's Principle, and fluid motion. Four main concept groups:

1. **Fluid Pressure and Buoyancy** — pressure defined as force per unit area (P = F/A); pascal as the SI unit. Buoyant force = the upward force exerted by a fluid on any object in it. Archimedes' Principle: buoyant force equals the weight of the fluid displaced. Float/sink rule: if buoyant force ≥ object's weight, the object floats.
2. **Density and Floating** — float/sink determined by comparing densities. Objects denser than the fluid sink; less dense objects float. Ship example: hollow shape reduces overall density even though steel > water density. Swim bladder biology integration.
3. **Pascal's Principle** — a change in pressure in an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally to all parts. Hydraulic devices multiply force: small force on small area → same pressure → larger force on large area. Worked math example: hydraulic lift (F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂).
4. **Fluids in Motion** — fluids move faster through narrow areas than wide areas (constant flow rate). Viscosity = resistance to flow; stronger particle attraction = higher viscosity. Bernoulli's Principle: as fluid speed increases, fluid pressure decreases.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A hydraulic car lift uses Pascal's Principle. The small input piston has an area of 10.5 cm². The large output piston has an area of 400 cm². A mechanic pushes on the small piston with a force of 500 N.
>
> **Part A — Calculate:** What force does the large piston exert? Show your work using Pascal's Principle (F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂).
>
> **Part B — Explain:** Why is the output force larger than the input force? Does this violate the Law of Conservation of Energy? Explain using Pascal's Principle and what you know about the distances the pistons travel.
>
> *(Part A: show equation + substitution + answer with units. Part B: 2–3 sentences; open own notes; graded on: correct calculation, correct explanation of pressure transmission, conservation of energy acknowledged)*

**Scoring note:** Part A answer: F₂ = (500 N × 400 cm²) / 10.5 cm² ≈ 19,048 N ≈ 19,000 N. Part B: the output force is larger because the same pressure acts on a larger area; energy is conserved because the large piston travels a much shorter distance than the small piston (work = force × distance stays equal). Students who can only state Pascal's Principle without calculating earn partial credit on Part A; the explanation in Part B targets the PS2-2 "mathematical representation" requirement.

**Why this prompt works:** It hits HS-PS2-2 directly (mathematical representation of pressure/force on objects) and pairs calculation with conceptual justification. The worked example from the textbook (same numbers) appears in their notes from Chunk B — this prompt is a transfer task, not a copy task.

## 4 — In-class lab block (§3.3 day, ~10 min)

**QuickLab — Density and Shape (opening of 03_3.md clip)**

- Materials: two equal golf-ball-sized pieces of clay per student, container of water
- Procedure: drop first clay ball into water and record observations; flatten second ball into a canoe/boat shape; place the clay boat gently into the water and record observations
- Analysis: (1) How does changing shape affect the buoyant force? (2) How is the change in shape related to the overall density of the boat?

**Cornell note integration:** After the lab, students write: "Density determines float/sink. Shape changes volume, which changes _____, which changes _____ force." This bridges the lab observation to the Chunk A reading.

**Optional demo — Cartesian Diver (inquiry lab, ~5 min):**
- Materials: 2L bottle, medicine dropper, water — trivially available
- Fill bottle with water, insert partially water-filled dropper, cap tightly; squeeze bottle
- Observe dropper sinking as water compresses into it; release to float again
- Connect: pressure increase transmitted through fluid → density of dropper changes → sinks; links to Pascal's Principle and submarine ballast tanks (Why It Matters sidebar)
- Run as an opening hook before the pre-teach intro
