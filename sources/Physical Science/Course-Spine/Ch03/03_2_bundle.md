## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**  
§3.2 applies the kinetic theory from §3.1 to explain what happens when matter changes state — and introduces the two conservation laws. Two main concept groups:

1. **Energy and Changes of State** — all six changes of state and their energy direction. Energy-adding changes (melting, evaporation/boiling, sublimation) require energy input to break particle attractions. Energy-releasing changes (freezing, condensation) release energy to surroundings. The *boiling point* and *melting point* are defined. Sweating as a real-world evaporation example. The critical concept: during a phase change, energy is being transferred but temperature does NOT change — particles are breaking attractions, not speeding up.
2. **Conservation Laws** — Law of Conservation of Mass (mass cannot be created or destroyed; burning match example) and Law of Conservation of Energy (energy cannot be created or destroyed; lawn mower/gasoline example). Both laws apply to physical and chemical changes.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> You are heating a pot of water from room temperature to steam. At 100°C, the water starts to boil — but no matter how long you keep the heat on, the temperature of the boiling water stays at 100°C while liquid water is still present.
>
> **Claim:** Does this violate the Law of Conservation of Energy? Explain what is happening to the energy being added during boiling, and describe what is happening to the water's particles at the molecular level.
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: claim that energy is NOT destroyed, correct explanation of phase-change plateau — energy breaks particle attractions rather than raising temperature, use of kinetic theory)*

**Scoring note:** Target answer: energy is being used to break the attractions between water molecules (changing state from liquid to gas), not to increase particle speed — so temperature (average KE) stays constant while energy is still being transferred. Conservation of energy is not violated because the energy is going into the state change. Students who only write "energy cannot be created or destroyed" without explaining where the energy goes earn partial credit. The phase-change graph (`Change of state graph for water.pdf`) shown in class will directly support this answer.

**Why this prompt works:** It hits HS-PS3-1 (energy representation during phase change) directly and requires students to connect the plateau concept from the graph to the particle-level explanation from the kinetic theory notes. Notes from both Chunk B and Chunk C are needed.

## 4 — In-class lab block (Boiling Lab — Chapter 3 primary lab, ~50 min)

**Lab — Boiling Lab (Chapter 3 Lab Modified - Final.docx)**

This is the chapter's primary lab and the direct hands-on companion to §3.2. Run it on the §3.2 teaching day or the day immediately after.

- Materials: hot plate, 100 mL water in 250–400 mL beaker, thermometer, clamp + ring stand, stopwatch, graph paper (all confirmed on hand per INVENTORY)
- Procedure: heat water from room temperature; record temperature every 30 seconds for ~18 minutes; identify the boiling onset temperature; graph temperature vs. time; note the plateau
- Analysis questions target: (1) identifying the plateau, (2) explaining why temperature stops rising during boiling using conservation of energy, (3) connecting the graph shape to the kinetic theory

**Cornell note integration:** The lab graph (temperature vs. time) goes directly into the lab report / data table. Students annotate the graph: "plateau starts here — energy is breaking particle attractions, not increasing speed." This annotation bridges the lab data to the CER from today.

**Lab debrief CER connection:** After the lab, have students look back at today's open-notes CER. Ask: *"Did your graph support or refute the answer you wrote? Where on the graph can you see the conservation of energy in action?"*

**Timing note:** The modified lab (one-period, boiling only) takes ~50 min. The full publisher lab (datasheet_a through _c) adds a freezing half using rock salt + coffee can — that is a two-period version. The modified version is the standard choice.
