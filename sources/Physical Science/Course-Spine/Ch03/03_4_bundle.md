## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**  
§3.4 establishes the three gas laws and their mathematical relationships. Three main concept groups:

1. **Boyle's Law** — at constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely related (P₁V₁ = P₂V₂). As pressure increases, volume decreases and vice versa. Worked example: balloon gas volume at two pressures.
2. **Gay-Lussac's Law** — at constant volume, pressure and temperature are directly related. As temperature increases, particle collisions increase, so pressure increases. Real-world example: tire pressure higher in summer than in winter; spray cans should be kept away from heat.
3. **Charles's Law** — at constant pressure, volume and temperature are directly related. As temperature increases, gas particles move faster and push outward → volume increases. Real-world example: inflated balloon shrinks in liquid nitrogen, expands when warmed.

The section includes a graphing skills feature: interpreting the linear (direct) graph for Charles's Law vs. the curved (inverse) graph for Boyle's Law, connecting graph shape to the type of relationship.

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> You check the pressure in your car tires on a cold winter morning (−10°C) and find they are underinflated. You remember that last fall, when it was 20°C, the tires were properly inflated. The volume of the tire has not changed.
>
> **Name the gas law that explains this observation.** Then explain, using that law and the kinetic theory of matter, why tire pressure drops when temperature decreases. Finally, predict what would happen to the tire pressure if the car sat in the sun on a hot summer day (40°C).
>
> *(3–5 sentences; open own notes; graded on: correct gas law named (Gay-Lussac's Law), kinetic theory explanation of particle collisions + temperature, correct directional prediction for summer)*

**Scoring note:** Target: Gay-Lussac's Law — at constant volume, pressure decreases as temperature decreases because gas particles move slower and collide with the tire walls less frequently and with less energy. Summer prediction: pressure increases. Students who name Boyle's Law (wrong — volume is constant here) or Charles's Law (wrong — volume is the variable in Charles's) have not distinguished the three laws; this is the most common error and the prompt is designed to expose it. The CFU 3.4.docx (created 2026-06-11 per report card) uses the same scenario as Q5 — this CER is the open-notes version; the CFU Q5 is the closed-notes follow-up.

**Why this prompt works:** It hits HS-PS1-5 territory (gas behavior under changing conditions) using a real-world scenario that distinguishes all three laws. Students need their notes to correctly identify Gay-Lussac's vs. the others. The kinetic-theory explanation requires connecting Ch3 §1 (particle motion → temperature) to §3.4 (pressure as collisions).

## 4 — In-class lab block (§3.4 day, ~30 min)

**Inquiry Lab — How Are Temperature and Volume Related? (embedded in 03_4.md clip)**

This lab directly demonstrates Charles's Law and belongs on the §3.4 day.

- Materials: aluminum pan, hot plate, ice water, 250 mL beaker, balloon (blown up to fill the beaker), stopwatch (~30 min total)
- Procedure: (1) Blow balloon inside beaker so it fills but doesn't extend past the rim; tie at opening. (2) Place beaker + balloon in ice water; record observations. (3) Remove and observe for several minutes. (4) Place beaker + balloon in hot water; record observations.
- Analysis questions: (1) How did changing temperature affect the balloon's volume? (2) Is the density of a gas affected by temperature? Explain.

**Cornell note integration:** After the lab, students write: "Charles's Law: when temperature ↑ at constant pressure, volume ___. My evidence from the lab: ___." This integrates the lab observation directly into the notes they will use for the CER.

**Connection to gas laws:** The lab demonstrates Charles's Law (temperature ↔ volume at constant pressure). Pair with a teacher demonstration of Gay-Lussac's Law if available (e.g., aerosol can warm/cool demo) to give all three laws an observable anchor.

**Timing note:** The lab takes ~30 min. Run after Chunk B (Gay-Lussac's Law) and before Chunk C (Charles's Law) so the lab observation precedes the formal statement of Charles's Law — students arrive at the law from data, not the reverse.
