## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§6.3 is the naming and formula section — the tool-building period of Chapter 6. Three concept groups:

1. **Naming Ionic Compounds** — cation name first (same as element name); anion name ends in -ide. Charge balance: sum of all ion charges must equal zero, which determines the ion ratio (e.g., CaF₂ because Ca²⁺ + 2F⁻ = 0). Transition metals form multiple cation charges (Fe²⁺ in FeO; Fe³⁺ in Fe₂O₃), identified by a Roman numeral in parentheses after the element name. A worked math-skills example walks through writing the formula for aluminum fluoride (AlF₃) using least-common-multiple logic.
2. **Naming Covalent Compounds** — use numerical prefixes (mono-/di-/tri-/tetra-/penta-/hexa-/hepta-/octa-/nona-/deca-) to indicate the count of each element. The element farther left on the periodic table is named first; the second element ends in -ide. If there is only one atom of the first element, no prefix is used. Examples: BF₃ = boron trifluoride; N₂O₄ = dinitrogen tetroxide.
3. **Empirical Formulas and Molecular Formulas** — an *empirical formula* gives the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound (e.g., H₂O for water, CH for benzene, CH₂O for formaldehyde/acetic acid/glucose all alike). A *molecular formula* gives the actual atom count per molecule (C₆H₁₂O₆ for glucose). Ionic compound formulas ARE the empirical formula. A mass-to-moles calculation yields empirical formulas from experimental data.

## 2 — Cue-column questions (Cornell left column)

*Students write the Q before reading the chunk; answer at the stop; summarize at the bottom.*

### Chunk A — Naming Ionic Compounds
1. In an ionic compound name, which ion is named first — the cation or the anion?
2. How does an anion's name differ from its element name? (What suffix is added?)
3. What rule determines the ratio of ions in an ionic compound (e.g., why CaF₂ and not CaF)?
4. Iron can form Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺. How do you show which iron ion is in the compound when you name it?
5. How do you determine the charge on a transition metal cation when you're given the compound's formula?

### Chunk B — Naming Covalent Compounds
6. What does the prefix *di-* mean? *Tetra-*? *Penta-*?
7. Which element is named first in a covalent compound? Which gets the -ide suffix?
8. When do you NOT use a prefix for the first element in a covalent compound name?
9. Name N₂O₄ using the rules from Chunk B. Show your reasoning step by step.

### Chunk C — Empirical and Molecular Formulas
10. What does an empirical formula tell you? What does a molecular formula tell you?
11. Formaldehyde (CH₂O), acetic acid (C₂H₄O₂), and glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) all have the same empirical formula. What is it? Why can different compounds share an empirical formula?
12. A compound contains 62 g of phosphorus and 80 g of oxygen per mole. How do you convert these masses to find the empirical formula? (Summarize the steps, not the full calculation.)

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> A chemist analyzes two iron-oxygen compounds. Compound X is FeO. Compound Y is Fe₂O₃. Both contain iron and oxygen, but they are different compounds with different properties.
>
> **Using your notes from today**, explain: (1) what charge the iron ion carries in each compound, showing your charge-balance reasoning, and (2) write the correct systematic name for each compound using the Roman numeral method.
>
> Write 3–5 sentences. Open own notes.
>
> *(Graded on: charge calculations shown or explained, correct Roman numerals applied, both names written correctly)*

**Scoring note (for Forms [short-answer] mode):** Brisk grades on three criteria. Key answer: FeO — one Fe²⁺ and one O²⁻; charges: (+2) + (−2) = 0; name = iron(II) oxide. Fe₂O₃ — three O²⁻ = −6 total; two Fe must supply +6; each Fe = +3; name = iron(III) oxide. Students who can't find the charge-balance method in their Chunk A notes will not be able to derive the charges.

**Why this prompt works:** The transition metal charge method is the most procedurally demanding skill in §6.3, and no existing CFU in Ch6 asks students to apply it (per the report card). This CER fills that gap directly. It also requires both calculation and communication — SEP-5 in application.

## 4 — Bell-ringer CFU (NEXT DAY, closed notes)

*Closed-notes recall at the start of §6.4 day.*

- A. Name these ionic compounds: (1) NaCl, (2) MgO, (3) FeCl₃. For #3, identify the charge on iron.
- B. Name these covalent compounds: (1) CO₂, (2) N₂O, (3) CCl₄. What prefix rule did you use for each?
- C. (Harder) A compound has the molecular formula C₆H₆. Its empirical formula is CH. Explain in one sentence what the difference between these two formulas tells you about this compound.

*In Forms mode: A and B are [short-answer] (answer-checkable); C is [short-answer]. On paper: quick to grade. Expected answers — A: sodium chloride; magnesium oxide; iron(III) chloride (Fe has 3+ charge: 3 × −1 = −3, so Fe = +3); B: carbon dioxide; dinitrogen monoxide; carbon tetrachloride; C: CH is the simplest ratio (1:1), but the actual molecule has 6 of each atom — the molecular formula is 6× the empirical formula.*
