## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§6.2 is the mechanistic heart of Chapter 6 and the primary delivery point for HS-PS1-2. Four sub-topics:

1. **Why Do Chemical Bonds Form?** — atoms bond to fill their outermost (s and p) orbitals, reaching a stable noble-gas electron configuration. This is the WHY that HS-PS1-2 requires. Two basic bond types are introduced: ionic and covalent.
2. **Ionic Bonds** — metal atoms lose electrons (→ cations) and nonmetal atoms gain electrons (→ anions); opposite charges attract and form an ionic network. Ionic solids do NOT conduct electricity (ions locked in place), but they conduct when melted or dissolved (ions free to move). Generally high melting/boiling points.
3. **Covalent Bonds** — nonmetal atoms share electrons so both reach a full outer level. Single bonds = 2 shared electrons; double bonds = 4; triple bonds = 6. Triple > double > single in bond strength. Nonpolar covalent (same element, equal sharing — Cl₂) vs. polar covalent (different elements, unequal sharing — H₂O). Metallic bonds: nuclei attract electrons so strongly the electrons are delocalized across many nuclei — this is why metals conduct electricity and are malleable.
4. **Polyatomic Ions** — groups of covalently bonded atoms that carry a charge as a unit (OH⁻, CO₃²⁻, NH₄⁺). They behave like single ions in ionic compounds. Parentheses in formulas group atoms of a polyatomic ion; subscript outside applies to the whole group. Oxygen-containing polyatomic anions end in -ite (fewer O) or -ate (more O).

### Chunk C — Polar/Nonpolar Covalent + Metallic Bonds

### Chunk D — Polyatomic Ions

12. What do parentheses mean in a chemical formula like (NH₄)₂SO₄?

## 3 — Daily open-notes CER (graded today, open own notes)

> **Prompt:**
>
> Sodium (Na) has 1 valence electron. Chlorine (Cl) has 7 valence electrons. Both atoms are unstable in their elemental form.
>
> **Using your notes from today**, explain step by step how Na and Cl bond to form sodium chloride (NaCl). In your explanation: (1) state what happens to Na's valence electron, (2) explain what happens to Cl as a result, (3) identify the type of bond that forms and why, and (4) connect this back to the reason atoms bond in the first place.
>
> Write 3–5 sentences. Open own notes.
>
> *(Graded on: electron transfer step described, correct bond type identified, "fill outer level" reasoning stated, claim is a complete explanation not just a list)*

**Scoring note (for Forms [short-answer] mode):** Brisk grades on four criteria above. Key answer: Na transfers 1 electron to Cl; Na becomes Na⁺ (stable, empty outer level like Ne); Cl becomes Cl⁻ (gains 1 electron, full outer level like Ar); opposite charges attract → ionic bond; this satisfies the driving force (fill outermost energy level). Students who can't find "fill outermost energy level" in their Chunk A notes will guess. Students with Chunk A notes can answer completely.

**Why this prompt works:** This is the report card's highest-priority CER bolt-on for the chapter. The NaCl electron transfer sequence is exactly the HS-PS1-2 application chain: outermost electron state → bond type prediction → compound formation. No other item in Ch6 asks students to complete this chain in writing. Thin notes → incomplete answer; detailed Chunk A notes → full credit.
