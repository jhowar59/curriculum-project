## 1 — Synopsis (teacher one-pager)

**What this section covers:**
§6.4 extends Chapter 6's bonding concepts into carbon chemistry and the molecules of life. Four sub-topics:

1. **Organic Compounds** — defined as covalently bonded compounds that contain carbon (excluding carbonates and oxides). Most also contain hydrogen; oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, and phosphorus can also appear. Examples are everywhere: aspirin (C₉H₈O₄), sorbitol, aspartame.
2. **Hydrocarbons + Alcohols** — *hydrocarbons* contain only carbon and hydrogen; *alkanes* have single bonds only (methane CH₄ simplest; n-alkanes = straight chain; isomers = same formula, different arrangement); *alkenes* have at least one C=C double bond (simplest = ethene C₂H₄). *Alcohols* contain the hydroxyl group (–OH); methanol (CH₃OH) and ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) are examples. Alcohols have higher boiling points than similar alkenes because –OH groups form hydrogen bonds with each other.
3. **Polymers** — large molecules made of repeating smaller units called *monomers*. Natural polymers: wood, cotton. Synthetic polymers: plastics, nylon, polypropylene. Elastic polymers return to original shape after stretching.
4. **Biochemical Compounds** — organic compounds made by living organisms. Three types: *carbohydrates* (C, H, O; small = sugars; large = chains of sugars; body breaks to simple sugars for fuel); *proteins* (chains of amino acids; each amino acid contains –NH₂ and –COOH groups; 20 amino acids found in naturally occurring proteins; body breaks dietary proteins to amino acids, then re-bonds them as needed); *DNA* (nucleotides in a double helix; 4 bases; carries genetic information).

>
> **Using your notes from today**, explain why ethanol has a much higher boiling point than ethene. Your explanation must identify (1) the structural difference between the two molecules, (2) the type of force that causes the boiling point difference, and (3) connect this back to a concept from Chapter 6 §6.1 or §6.2.
>
> Write 3–5 sentences. Open own notes.
>
> *(Graded on: hydroxyl group identified as structural difference, hydrogen bonding named as the relevant force, explicit connection to structure → properties chain or polar covalent bonding from earlier sections)*

**Scoring note (for Forms [short-answer] mode):** Brisk grades on three criteria. Key answer: ethanol has a –OH hydroxyl group; the polar O–H bond creates hydrogen bond attractions between molecules; these must be broken for ethanol to boil, requiring more energy (higher temperature) than for non-polar ethene. Connection back: polar covalent bonds (§6.2-C) cause partial charges that enable hydrogen bonding (§6.1-B).

**Why this prompt works:** The §6.4 CER closes the chapter arc — students apply *polar covalent → hydrogen bonding → property difference*, which spans three earlier sub-sections. It tests whether the bonding concepts from §§6.1–6.2 are functional in a new context. The –OH group is the pivot; students who wrote the §6.1 hydrogen bond definition in their notes can connect it here.

## 4 — In-class lab block (§6.4 day, ~30 min)

**Inquiry Lab — What Properties Does a Polymer Have? (p. 204, textbook)**
- **Materials:** borax (4 g), white glue, two 250 mL beakers, water, plastic spoon, plastic sandwich bag
- **Procedure overview:**
  1. Mix 4 g borax with 100 mL water in Beaker 1; stir to dissolve.
  2. In Beaker 2, mix equal parts white glue and water (diluted glue volume should be 100–200 mL total).
  3. Pour borax solution into diluted glue; stir with plastic spoon until too thick to stir.
  4. Remove material and knead with gloved hands; store in plastic bag.
- **Analysis questions (from clip):**
  1. What happens to the new material when it is stretched or rolled into a ball and bounced?
  2. Compare the properties of the glue with the properties of the new material.
  3. The properties of the new material result from bonds between borax and glue particles. If too little borax were used, how would properties differ?
  4. Does the new material have the properties of a polymer? Explain.

**Cornell note integration:** Students record the definition of *polymer*, *monomer*, and *elastic* from Chunk B notes, then use these definitions to answer Analysis Q4 in 2 sentences — this IS the note they'll draw on for the closed-notes CFU.

**Equipment note:** Borax is available in most school supply rooms (laundry section; 20 Mule Team brand). White glue (Elmer's) required. Low-cost, low-hazard lab. Gloves and apron recommended.
